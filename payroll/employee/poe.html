Determine the province of employment (POE)

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language.

The changes identified by the tags are effective January 1, 2024 and is identified with New CRA administrative policy.

When you pay employment income such as salaries, wages, or commissions, the employee's province or territory of employment (POE) must be determined so that the proper deductions are withheld.

An establishment of the employer is any place or premises in Canada that is owned, leased or rented by an employer where employees report to work or from which employees are paid. For purposes of POE, this does not need to be a permanent establishment.

Income tax legislated by the province or territory

CRA's mandate is to administer provincial and territorial personal income tax as legislated by the province or territory. Any concerns regarding the tax policy of a province or territory should be directed to the applicable provincial or territorial Ministry of Finance.

POE based on employment income

You need to determine the POE based on the type of income, your employee residency status and your residency status.

If the you don't know the residency status

The individual may be uncertain of their residency status.

The residency status is determined on a case-by-case basis and an individual's whole situation. If you want the CRA's opinion on your residency status, fill out one of the following:

  • Form NR74, Determination of Residency Status (Entering Canada)
  • Form NR73, Determination of Residency Status (Leaving Canada)

Learn more about the residency status of an:

  • Individual: Individuals – Leaving or entering Canada and non-residents
  • Employer: Businesses – International and non-resident taxes
How to ask for a binding determination

Individuals may ask the Income Tax Rulings Directorate (ITRD) to issue a binding advance income tax ruling about their residency status. You may have to pay a fee for ITRD to issue the ruling.

Employee is a resident of Canada

The CRA considers the employee to be reporting to the establishment of the employer when they physically report there. There is no minimum amount of time the employee has to report to that place.

An employee that does not physically report for work is considered to be reporting for work at the establishment of the employer when they can be reasonably regarded as attached to the establishment.

What is considered attached to the establishment of the employer

In order of importance, the criteria to determine if the employee can be reasonably considered to be attached to the establishment of the employer are:

Option 1 - Jeremiah?

  1. The establishment where the employee would, if there were no home teleworking agreement, physically come to work in person to carry out the functions related to their employment duties.
  2. The establishment where the employee goes or would go, if there were no home teleworking agreement, to physically attend meetings, through any type of communication.
  3. The establishment where the employee goes or would go, if there were no home teleworking agreement, to physically receive work-related material or equipment or associated instructions and assistance.
  4. The establishment where the employee would, if there were no home teleworking agreement, physically come in person to receive instructions from their employer regarding their duties, through any type of communication.
  5. The establishment from which the employee is hired, as indicated in the employment contract or letter of offer.
  6. The nature of the duties performed by the employee.

If an employee neither reports physically to an establishment of their employer or can be considered attached to one of their employer's establishments after review of the above criteria, they are considered to not report to an establishment of the employer.

Situations

Situation: Employee reports for work (physically or is considered attached) to only 1 establishment of the employer in Canada New CRA administrative policy

The province of employment is where the employee reports for work.

There is no minimum amount of time the employee has to report to that place.

    Appropriate payroll deductions tables

Situation: Employee reports for work (physically) to more than 1 establishment of the employer in Canada during a pay period

The province of employment is the province in which the establishment of the employer is located where the employee had either:

  • Spent the most time
  • Worked last, if the employee reported to each establishment for the same amount of time

    TD1 federal form

Situation: Employee works in Canada but never reports for work (never physically and not considered attached) to an establishment of the employer

The province of employment is the province or territory where the employer's establishment is located from which the employee's salary is paid. Generally, this is the location of the employer’s payroll department or payroll records.

Situation: Employee works in Canada but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

The employment is considered in Canada but beyond the limits of any province or territory.

Employee is not a resident of Canada for the full year

Where the employee is not a Canadian resident for the whole year, the province of employment will change depending on their specific residency status.

Situations

Situation: Employee is a non-resident, including a commuter

The province of employment is where employment duties are performed if one of the following situation apply:

  • Employee reports for work at an establishment of the employer in Canada
  • Employee works in Canada, but does not report for work at an establishment of the employer
  • Employee works in Canada, but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

Use:

    TD1 federal form

Situation: Employee is a part-year resident

For the part of the year that the employee is either:

  • A resident of Canada, continue to: Employee is a resident of Canada
  • Not a resident of Canada, continue to: Employee is a non-resident, including a commuter
Situation: Employee is a deemed resident or sojourner

The employment is considered in Canada but beyond the limits of any province or territory if one of the following situation apply:

  • Employee reports for work at an establishment of the employer in Canada
  • Employee works in Canada, but does not report for work at an establishment of the employer
  • Employee works in Canada, but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

POE based on non-employment income
(for example, pension income, retiring allowances, RRSP and CPP/QPP)

The province of employment is the recipient's province or territory of residence if you pay amounts other than employment income, such as pension income, retiring allowance, or RRSP.
