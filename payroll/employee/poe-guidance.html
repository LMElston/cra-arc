Guidance on how to determine the province of employment (POE)

Content has been developed to provide guidance only. The guidance does not replace the new CRA administrative policy and does not replace the law.

You need to consider in order of importance the criteria to determine if an employee is considered to be attached to the establishment of the employer. This must be done based on a thorough review of the facts in each specific case.

Gather the facts

Answer a few questions based on the following factors to help you gather the facts.

The results from each question need to be viewed as a whole in order to help you decide. This also needs to be done on a case-by-case basis.

Type of income

You need to determine the POE based on the type of income and the residency status .

What is the type of income?

  • Employment income
  • Non-employment income (for example: pension income, retiring allowances, RRSP and CPP/QPP)

Residency status

What is the residency status?

  • Employee is a resident of Canada
  • Employee is not a resident of Canada for the full year

Employee is a resident of Canada

Employee is not a resident of Canada for the full year

Where the employee is not a Canadian resident for the whole year, the province of employment will change depending on their specific residency status.

What is the situation of the employee?

  • Non-resident, including a commuter
  • Part-year resident
  • Deemed resident or sojourner

Employee is a non-resident, including a commuter

p>The province of employment is where employment duties are performed if one of the following situation apply:

  • Employee reports for work at an establishment of the employer in Canada
  • Employee works in Canada, but does not report for work at an establishment of the employer
  • Employee works in Canada, but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables

Learn more: Deemed residents of Canada

Employee is part-year resident

For the part of the year that the employee is either:

  • A resident of Canada, continue to: Employee is a resident of Canada
  • Not a resident of Canada, continue to: Employee is a non-resident, including a commuter

Employee is a deemed resident or sojourner

The employment is considered in Canada but beyond the limits of any province or territory if one of the following situation apply:

  • Employee reports for work at an establishment of the employer in Canada
  • Employee works in Canada, but does not report for work at an establishment of the employer
  • Employee works in Canada, but employer does not have an establishment in Canada

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • Payroll deductions tables for In Canada beyond the limits of any province/territory or outside Canada

Do not use:

  • TD1 provincial and territorial form

Learn more: Deemed residents of Canada

POE for non-employment income
(for example, pension income, retiring allowances, RRSP and CPP/QPP)

The province of employment is the recipient's province or territory of residence if you pay amounts other than employment income, such as pension income, retiring allowance, or RRSP.

Use:

  • TD1 federal form
  • TD1 provincial and territorial form
  • Appropriate payroll deductions tables

Did the employer place restrictions on the use of the benefit?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Employer is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employer is the primary beneficiary if the employer places restrictions (for example, where and how the benefit can be used) on the use of the benefit.

This may mean that the employer is trying to contain the use to that which is beneficial to the employer.

Indicator: Employee is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employee is the primary beneficiary if the employer did not place restrictions on the ability of the employee to use the benefit for personal use

Did the nature of the benefit significantly limit the opportunity for personal use?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Employer is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employer is the primary beneficiary if the benefit provides limited opportunity for personal use.

This may be considered for business use.

Indicator: Employee is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employee is the primary beneficiary if the benefit does not provide a limited opportunity for personal use.

Use in employment

Did the employee's work productivity improve because of the benefit?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Employer is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employer is the primary beneficiary if the benefit improves the employee's work productivity. This may depend in part on the employee's particular role and responsibilities.

This is because the benefit may considered to be tied to the employee's specific employment duties.

Indicator: Employee is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employee is the primary beneficiary.

Would it be possible for the employee to do their job without the benefit or a replacement?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Employee is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employee is the primary beneficiary if the employee would have used or purchased the benefit regardless of their specific duties.

This is because the benefit may considered not tied to the employee's employment duties.

Indicator: Employer is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employer is the primary beneficiary if the job duties being fulfilled would not be possible without the benefit.

This is because the benefit may considered to be tied to the employee's employment duties.

Is it usual for employers in this industry to provide this benefit to employees to allow those employees to more effectively perform the duties of employment?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Employer is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employer is the primary beneficiary if it is common in the industry to provide the benefit to allow employees to more effectively perform their duties.

This is because the benefit may considered to be tied to the employee's specific employment duties.

Indicator: Employee is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employee is the primary beneficiary.

This is because the benefit may considered not tied to the employee's specific employment duties.

Recipient

Was the benefit available to other employees with different duties (or all employees at a same pay level or at a certain seniority)?

  • Yes
  • No

Indicator: Employee is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employee is the primary beneficiary if the benefit is given to other employees with different duties.

This could mean that the benefit was not tied to the employee's specific employment duties.

Indicator: Employer is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employer is the primary beneficiary if the benefit is not made available to other employees with different duties than the employee.

This could mean that the benefit is tied to the employee's specific employment duties.

Benefit

Is the benefit something that can be used on an ongoing basis or something that can only be used once?

  • Ongoing
  • Once

Indicator: Employee is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employee is the primary beneficiary if the benefit can be used on an ongoing basis.

Indicator: Employer is the primary beneficiary

When combined with other factors, this may indicate the employer is the primary beneficiary if the benefit is used only once.

This could mean that the benefit is tied to the employee's specific employment duties.

Analyze the facts

You need to analyze the facts by organizing and assessing the information you gathered. This process will move the worker into one side of the continuum, and confirm if the employee or the employer is the primary beneficiary of the benefit.

You must analyze the answer to each question from both the employee and the employer to help you decide if the evidence moves the employee or the employer is the primary beneficiary of the benefit side of the continuum.

  • Employee is the primary beneficiary
  • Employer is the primary beneficiary

Weighing the facts

This is the most important part of analyzing the information. When reviewing the factors, there may be some facts that support the benefit is taxable or not.

Case-by-case basis

Whether the employee or the employer is the primary beneficiary of the benefit needs to be analyzed on a case-by-case basis. Each case is unique and requires a thorough review of the facts. It is also important to remember that when attempting to move the employee or the employer as the primary beneficiary of the benefit to one side of the continuum or the other, you must examine all factors. There is no one factor that is determinative, and there is a non-exhaustive list of factors to consider.
