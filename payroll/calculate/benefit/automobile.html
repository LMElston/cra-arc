Automobile provided by the employer

You may be looking for:

  • Automobile or motor vehicle benefits – Allowances or reimbursements provided to an employee for the use of their own vehicle
  • Motor vehicle provided by the employer

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may provide an automobile to your employee or a shareholder, to use in performing their duties of office or employment. Generally, the personal use of the employer provided automobile is a taxable benefit.

  • Employer
    Includes a person related to the employer
  • Employee and shareholder
    Includes a person related to the employee or shareholder

Steps

  1. Determine if the motor vehicle is an automobile

    The Income Tax Act defines automobiles and motor vehicles differently for tax purposes.

    Answer a few questions to determine if the vehicle is an automobile or a motor vehicle.

    Launch vehicle validator

    Vehicle validator

    Automobile and motor vehicle definitions

    The difference between an automobile or a motor vehicle depends on:

    • Seating capacity
    • Type of vehicle
    • Primary use of the vehicle

    As per the Income Tax Act:

    • An automobile is defined as a motor vehicle that is designed or adapted mainly to carry individuals and their baggage on highways and streets, and has a seating capacity of not more than the driver and eight passengers. Vans and family cars belong to this category. Vehicles used for emergency response and vehicles used mainly to earn income are excluded from the definition of the term automobile: for example, ambulances, taxis and hearses.
    • A motor vehicle is defined as an automotive vehicle designed or adapted to be used on highways and streets but does not include trolley buses (tramway or streetcar) and any vehicle that operates exclusively on rails.
    More than 8 passengers, plus a driver

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle.

    Less than 8 passengers, plus a driver

    The vehicle is considered a motor vehicle if one of the following apply:

    • Primarily (50% or more) of the distance driven as a taxi
    • Primarily (50% or more) of the distance driven as a business of transporting passengers
    • Bought to sell, rent, or lease in a sales, rental, or leasing business
    • An ambulance
    • Clearly marked as a police or fire emergency response vehicle
    • Clearly marked as an emergency medical response vehicle that you use to carry emergency medical equipment and one or more emergency medical attendants or paramedics
    • Van, pick-up truck or similar type vehicle (function and design similar to pick-up truck or van) that can be used for the transportation of goods, equipment or passengers to earn or produce income, and one of the following apply:
      • Back part of the vehicle adapted so that the vehicle is unable to carry passengers
      • Used 50% to 89% or more at a remote work location (at least 30 kilometres away from any community having a population of at least 40,000)
      • Used 90% or more (in the year it was bought or leased) to transport goods or equipment in the course of business
    • A hearse or other vehicle used in a funeral business to transport passengers

    A clearly marked emergency response vehicle means that the vehicle has decals and visible signs of being an emergency vehicle, including clearly marked police or fire vehicles and medical response vehicles, used to carry emergency medical equipment and one or more emergency medical attendants or paramedics.

    If the vehicle is not one of the above, it is considered an automobile.

    • If the vehicle is a motor vehicle, do not continue to next step. Refer to Motor vehicle provided by the employer
    • If the vehicle is an automobile, continue to Step 2 - Determine if you made the automobile available to the employee

  2. Determine if you made the automobile available to the employee

    An automobile is available to your employee if they have access to or control over the vehicle. This includes any part of a day, weekends and holidays during the calendar year.

    This applies as long as the kilometres driven by your employee were in the course of their employment duties and the vehicle is returned to your premises at the end of their work day.

    • If you did not make the automobile available to your employee, this is not a taxable benefit. Do not continue to next step.
    • If you made the automobile available to your employee, continue to Step 3 - Determine if there was personal driving of the automobile

  3. Determine if there was personal driving of the automobile

    Personal driving is any driving by an employee, or a person related to the employee, of an automobile provided by the employer for purposes not related to their employment.

    • Personal driving

      • Vacation trips
      • Driving to conduct personal activities
      • Travel between home and a regular place of employment  other than a point of call
      • Travel between home and a regular place of employment  even if you insist the employee drives the vehicle home, such as:
        • When the employee is on call
        • When the employer believes the vehicle and its contents are more secure at the employee’s residence
        • Where it is more convenient or economical to have the vehicle driven to the employee’s residence

    • Business driving

      • Travel between office workplace to client workplace
      • Travel between office workplace for errands for business purposes
      • Travel between home to client workplace
      • Travel between home to a point of call which is a place to perform employment duties, such as:
        • Salesperson visiting customers
        • Going to a client’s premises for a meeting
        • Making a repair call
    What is the regular place of employement

    The CRA considers the regular place of employment any location where your employee regularly:

    • Reports for work
    • Performs the duties of employment

    "Regularly" means the degree of frequency or repetition in the employee's reporting to a work location in a period (weekly, monthly or yearly) . This does not need to be your establishment.

    Examples

    For example:

    • Office where your employee reports daily
    • Several store locations that a manager visits monthly
    • Client's premises when your employee reports daily for a 6 month project
    • Client's premises when your employee attends biweekly meetings

    Employee drives between home to more than 1 location during the same day

    If your employee drives to multiple regular work locations and drives between home and several work locations, the personal driving during the same day is where the employee had either:

    • Spent the most time
    • Worked last, if the employee reported to each location for the same amount of time

    Any travel by the employee between work locations is business related.

    Examples - Automobile made available for personal and business driving
    Examples of automobile made available
    Examples Result

    Employee is a technician who works for a local telephone company. The company provides the employee with a service vehicle, unit 333. At the beginning of each work day, the employee uses unit 333 to conduct the service visits assigned to them. At the end of each day, the employee returns unit 333 to the company's parking lot and returns the keys to the employer.

    The benefit is not taxable to the employee because:

    • The automobile is made available during working hours
    • The automobile is only used for business purposes

    Employee is a regional manager for the same telephone company. Unit 222 is assigned to the employee. One of the requirements of the employee's position requires them to travel abroad on a regular basis. As a result, the employee keeps the keys to the vehicle throughout the year. When travelling abroad, the employee leaves unit 222 at the airport. The employee also uses the vehicle to travel between their home and their workplace.

    The benefit is taxable to the employee because:

    • The automobile is made available throughout the year
    • The automobile is used for business and personal purposes

    As a work incentive, the employer makes an automobile available to an employee's spouse, to be driven for personal use.

    The benefit is taxable to the employee (not the spouse) because:

    • The automobile is made available throughout the year
    • The automobile is used for personal purposes to an employee's spouse
    • If there is no personal driving, the use of the employer-provided automobile is not a taxable benefit. Do not continue to next step.
    • If there is personal driving, the use of the employer-provided automobile may be a taxable benefit. Continue to Step 4 - Determine if the benefit is taxable.

  4. Determine if the benefit is taxable

    Depending on the situation, the use of the employer-provided automobile may not be a taxable benefit.

    • Special situations

      If the the use of the employer-provided automobile is in one of the following special situation, do not continue to next step. Refer to:

      • Transportation from pickup points to an employment location where public and private vehicles are neither allowed nor practical at the location because of security or other reasons
      • Transportation to your employee who works in a prescribed zone
      • Transportation to your employee who has an severe or prolonged impairment
      • Transportation to your employee who works in a remote location
      • Transportation to your employee who works at a special work site

    • Taxable situations

      If the use of the employer-provided automobile is not in one of the above special situation, the use of the automobile is a taxable benefit.

      If you make an automobile available   to your employee and they use the automobile for both business and personal purposes  , the personal use is taxable. You need to calculate the value of the following:

      • Automobile standby charge benefit related to the availability and personal use
      • Automobile operating expense  benefit when you pay the operating costs of the automobile subject to a standby charge
      Examples of operating costs
      • Includes
        • Fuel
        • Oil
        • Maintenance costs
        • All repair costs, minus insurance payments
        • Licenses
        • Insurance costs, etc.
      • Does not include
        • Interest
        • Capital cost allowance (CCA) or leasing costs
        • Parking expenses

      Continue to Step 5 - Determine the required information (logbook) your employee must keep

  5. Determine the required information (logbook) your employee must keep

    If you make an automobile available  to your employee, the employee must keep a logbook of the trips made with the automobile. The logbook will support your calculation of the automotive benefit and any expenses the employee will claim. They must give you a copy.

    The employee must record the following information in a logbook or daily record:

    • Total number of days that the automobile was made available them during the year
    • Total number of kilometres travelled for business and personal use (on a daily, weekly or monthly basis) during the number of days that the automobile was made available.
    Logbook - Additional required information
    • Personal driving  required information

      If the automobile is used only for personal purposes, the employee must record the following information:

      • Total number of days that the automobile is made available to the employee (or to a person related to the employee) during the year
      • Kilometres registered on the odometer at the beginning and end of the year or of each period during which the automobile is made available, on a continuous basis, to the employee
    • Business driving  required information

      The employee must record, on a daily basis, for each trip made to perform their duties, the following information:

      • Place of departure and the place of destination
      • Number of kilometres travelled between those two places
      • Any other information to establish that the trip is made by the employee to perform their duties

    Continue to Step 6 - Determine if the benefit is taxable.

  6. Get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the automobile benefit

    Reminder: You can use Automobile Benefits Online Calculator that will calculate the benefit for you.

    How to calculate the value using estimates during the year

    During the year, you must calculate the value of the benefit using estimates. To obtain the amount you must add to the employee’s salary for a given pay period, you must divide the value of the benefit you have estimated by the number of pay periods in the year.

    At the end of the year, you must calculate the value of the benefit related to an automobile using the actual number of kilometres travelled during the year.

    You must get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the automobile benefit. Based on these records, you need to determine if you can use the reduced standby charge calculation.

    • If the employee kept the required detailed records and you received a copy, the benefit meets the condition 1 of 4 for the reduced standby charge.

    • If no record is kept, the employee does not benefit from the reduction.

  7. Determine if the employee is required to use the vehicle in connection with or in the course of an office or employment

    You need to determine if the employee was required to use the vehicle in connection with or in the course of an office or employment.

    Learn more about the factors to consider if you don't know who is the primary beneficiary: employee or employer .

    • If the employer required the employee to use the vehicle as part of the duties of an office or employment, the benefit meets condition 2 of 4 for the reduced standby charge.

    • If the employer did not require the employee to use the vehicle as part of the duties of an office or employment, the employee does not benefit from the reduction.

  10. Calculate the standby charge benefit

    If you make an automobile available   to your employee, you must calculate the value of the standby charge related to the personal use  and the availability of the automobile.

    Situations

    You own the automobile - Calculate using the simplified calculation

    You can use simplified calculation if all the following conditions are met:

    • You own the automobile provided to the employee
    • The employee uses the same automobile throughout the year
    • The employee's duties do not consist primarily in selling or leasing automobiles
    • The employee is not entitled to a reduction of the value of the standby charge

    Calculations

    What is included in the cost of the automobile

    The cost of the automobile includes all of the following:

    • Cost of the automobile when it was bought, including options and accessories, and the GST/HST and PST, but not including any reduction for a trade-in
    • Cost of additions, including the GST/HST and PST, made to the automobile after it was bought

    Where the automobile was purchased from a non-arm's length person, the cost is generally equal to the fair market value when it was bought, including options and GST/HST or PST.

    How to calculate the cost of the automobile when used part of an automobile fleet

    During the year, when your employee uses at least one automobile that is part of an automobile fleet that you operate, the guidelines below apply for calculating the cost of the automobile.

    You and your employee must both approve this calculation method.

    • If an automobile is assigned to your employee from a fleet or pool on a long-term or exclusive basis, you can calculate the standby charge based solely on the cost of the automobile assigned to the employee
    • f your employee uses more than one automobile and your fleet is mostly made up of similar automobiles, or if it can be grouped into a few similar groups, you can calculate the cost of the automobile based on the average cost of the group from which the automobile was provided to your employee.

    You lease the automobile - Calculate using the detailed calculation

    Use this calculation if you lease the automobile.

      Cost of automobile

      What is included in the cost of the lease

      The cost of leasing the automobile includes the following:

      • Rental cost for the automobile
      • Any associated costs, such as maintenance contracts, excess mileage charges, terminal charges less terminal credits
      • GST/HST and PST paid to the lessor under the leasing contract

      The cost of leasing the automobile does not include liability and collision insurance costs.

      What are lump-sum lease payments

      Some lease contracts may contain clauses that reduce the monthly lease costs by requiring a substantial lump-sum payment at the start or end of the lease. Care must be taken to determine if it is actually a lease payment, not a payment aimed at purchasing the vehicle, which could change the calculation of standby charge for the vehicle. Payments made at the start of a lease contract are lease payments for the year and apply in full to the calculation of the benefit. The CRA will give the employer the option of proportionally attributing such payments over the term of the lease and calculating the benefit accordingly.

      A variation of this approach is seen when an employer provides a substantial refundable deposit at the start of a lease contract, after which the monthly lease costs are reduced and the deposit is credited to the purchase of the vehicle at the end of the lease contract or refunded if the employer does not purchase the vehicle. The value of the lease, when calculating the benefit received by the employee from the use of the vehicle, is the fair market value of the lease, equivalent to what the lease payments would have been if the deposit did not exist.

      Lump-sum payments paid or received at the end of the lease are considered to be terminal charges or credits.

The initial costs (including down payment, trade-in amount, and taxes not included in monthly lease cost) was $500. The monthly leasing cost (including associated costs, GST/HST and PST) is $600 and the total lease period is 24 months. She is not selling or leasing automobiles. The automobile was made available to Judith for 150 days during the year. Judith was required to use the automobile in connection with her employment. She used the automobile for business purposes 40% of the time. In verifying her travel, you found that she drove 5,500 km for personal use 12,000 km out of a total of 20,000 km. Judith reimbursed you $500 for the standby charge.

      Cost of automobile

    • $500 is the one time charge for down payment
    • divide by 24 months is the duration of lease
    • equals $20.83 is the prorated amount of the one time charges
    • plus $600 is the monthly payment to lessor, including associated costs, GST/HST and PST
    • equals $620.83 is the result A for the total monthly leasing cost, including down payment, trade-in, and taxes

      • Use and availability

    • 150 is the total number of days when the automobile was made available to the employee
    • divide by 30 days
    • equals 5 is the result B for the use and availability (rounded to the nearest whole number)
    • multiply by $620.83 is the result A for the monthly leasing cost
    • equals $3,104.15 is the result
    • multiply by 2/3
    • equals $2,069.43 is the value of the standby charge benefit
    • minus $500 is the amounts your employee reimbursed you for the standby charge
    • equals $1,569.43 is the total value of the standby charge benefit

    If you make an automobile available   to your employee, you must also calculate the value of the operating costs  benefit related to the personal use  of the automobile.

    You can use the simplified calculation if all the following conditions are met:

    • The employee informed you in writing, before the end of the year, of his or her intention to use the simplified method

    • equals This is the amount for the operating costs benefit

    Employer's allowable employment expenses

    The type of vehicle you own can affect the calculation of the allowable employment expenses. You need to calculate the depreciable value depending on the type of automobile you are providing to your employee for business purposes.

    Determine the limit based on the type of vehicle and the expense

    The Income Tax Act restricts the amount of depreciation that can be apply if the vehicle cost exceeds a certain amount. This limit includes both new and used vehicles purchased on or after January 1, 2022 and applies to the cost of the vehicle before GST/HST and PST.

    The Act differentiates between passenger vehicles and other vehicles that may be used in a business. For example, taxis or pick-up trucks that are used primarily to transport goods or equipment as part of your regular business, do not have limits on what you can deduct for tax purposes.

    You can deduct the cost of the automobile depreciation (CCA). If you pay your employees mileage for the use of their vehicle for business purposes, there is also limit to the amount that you can reimburse those expenses and still be able to deduct the full reimbursement as an expense.

    Deductible limit for interest paid on amounts borrowed to purchase a vehicle $300 per month for loans related to vehicle
