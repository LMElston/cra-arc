Aircraft benefits - Personal use of aircraft owned or leased by the employer

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may require your employee, a shareholder, or a partner to use an aircraft to perform their duties of office or employment.

  • Employer
    Includes a person related to the employer
  • Employee and shareholder
    Includes a person related to the employee or shareholder

On this page

  • Steps
  • References

Steps

  1. Determine if you made the aircraft available to the employee

    An aircraft is available to your employee if they have access to or control over the aircraft. This includes any part of a day, weekends and holidays during the calendar year.

    • If you did not make the aircraft available to your employee, there is no taxable benefit to calculate. Do not continue to next step.
    • If you made the aircraft available to your employee, continue to Step 2 - Determine if there was personal driving of the aircraft.

  2. Determine if there was personal use of the aircraft

    Personal use is any use by an employee, or a person related to the employee, of an aircraft provided by the employer for purposes not related to their employment.

    If your employee does not use your aircraft for any personal use, there is no taxable benefit, even if the aircraft is available to your employee for the entire year. This applies as long as the flying hours by your employee were in the course of their employment duties and the aircraft is returned to your premises at the end of their work day.

    What is a regular place of employment

    The CRA considers the regular place of employment any location where your employee regularly:

    • Reports for work
    • Performs the duties of employment

    "Regularly" means the degree of frequency or repetition in the employee's reporting to a work location in a period (weekly, monthly or yearly) . This does not need to be your establishment.

    Generally, any travel by the employee between work and regular place of employment is considered personal driving.

    Examples

    For example:

    • Office where your employee reports daily
    • Several store locations that a manager visits monthly
    • Client's premises when your employee reports daily for a 6 month project
    • Client's premises when your employee attends biweekly meetings
    What if the employee flyes to multiple work locations during the same day

    The CRA considers that if your employee flyes to multiple work locations in a day, only the travel between the final work location and the employee's home is considered personal use.

    Any travel by the employee between work locations is considered business use.

    • If there is no personal flying, the use of the employer-provided aircraft is not a taxable benefit. Do not continue to next step.
    • If there is personal flying, the use of the employer-provided aircraft may be a taxable benefit. Continue to Step 3 - Determine if special considerations apply to the motor vehicle benefit.

  3. Determine if the aircraft benefit is taxable

    • If the benefit is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations. Do not continue to next step.
    • If the benefit is taxable, continue to Step 6 - Review the required information (logbook) your employee must have kept.

  4. Review the required information (logbook) your employee must have kept
