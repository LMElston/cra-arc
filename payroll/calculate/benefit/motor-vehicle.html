Motor vehicle provided by the employer

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may require your employee, a shareholder, or a partner to use a motor vehicle to perform their duties of office or employment. You may provide a motor vehicle to your employee or the employee may use their own.

  • Employer
    Includes a person related to the employer
  • Employee and shareholder
    Includes a person related to the employee or shareholder
  • Steps
  • References

Steps

  1. Determine if a motor vehicle is an automobile

    The CRA defines motor vehicles and automobiles differently for tax purposes.

    Answer a few questions to determine if the vehicle is an automobile or a motor vehicle.

    Launch vehicle validator

    vehicle validator

    • If the vehicle is an automobile, do not continue to next step. Refer to Automobile benefits - Automobile provided by the employer
    • If the vehicle is a motor vehicle, continue to Step 2 - Determine if the benefit is taxable

  2. Determine if the benefit is taxable

    Generally, if your employee uses a motor vehicle to perform their duties of office or employment, the benefit is taxable. Depending on your situation, the benefit may not be taxable under the CRA's administrative policy .

    Situations

    Situation: You own or lease the motor vehicle - You provide the motor vehicle to your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide your employee with a motor vehicle that you own or lease and your employee uses the motor vehicle for business use only, the benefit is not taxable.

    What is considered business use and personal use

    The personal use is when the employee's use of a motor vehicle provided by the employer or a person related to the employer, for purposes other than the employer's business.

    • Business use

      • Travel from office workplace to client workplace (and vice versa)
      • Travel from office workplace for errands for business purposes (and vice versa)
      • Travel from home to client workplace (and vice versa)

    • Personal use

      • Travel from office workplace to home (and vice versa), even if the employer insist the employee to drive the vehicle home, for example:
        • Employee is on 24 hour call
        • Employer feels the security of the vehicle and its contents is better served at the employee's residence
        • More convenient or economical to have the vehicle driven to the employee's residence
      • Vacation trips

    Taxable situation

    If you provide your employee with a motor vehicle that you own or lease and your employee uses the motor vehicle for both business and personal purposes, the personal use is taxable. You may need to calculate the value of the following:

    • Benefit related to the leasing of the motor vehicle
    • Operating costs  benefit
    Examples of operating costs
    • Includes
      • Fuel
      • Oil
      • Maintenance costs
      • All repair costs, minus insurance payments
      • Licenses
      • Insurance costs, etc.
    • Does not include
      • Interest
      • Capital cost allowance (CCA) or leasing costs
      • Parking expenses

    Continue to Step 3 - Get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the motor vehicle benefit.

    Situation: The self-employed individual or an individual who is a member of a partnership owns or leases a motor vehicle

    The information on this webpage does not apply to a self-employed individual or an individual who is a member of a partnership who personally owns or leases a motor vehicle to earn income from the business.

    Learn more:

    Situation: The employee owns the motor vehicle - You provide an allowance to your employee for motor vehicle expenses

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the Income Tax Act (ITA) exemption, if you paid an allowance to your employee to compensate for the operating costs of a motor vehicle they own, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • The allowance is calculated only on the actual number of kilometres for business purposes  (in connection with or during the course of the office or employment duties)
    • The allowance rate per-kilometre is reasonable , meaning:
      • The rate matches the reasonable allowance rate per-kilometre
      • If the rate does not match the reasonable allowance rate per-kilometre, you must be able to justify your position
    • You do not pay an allowance and also reimburse the employee for some or all of the expenses related to the use of the vehicle, unless the reimbursement is for supplementary business insurance, tolls or ferry charges not covered by the allowance
    Reasonable allowance rates for the use of motor vehicles (per kilometre)

    Generally, the per-kilometre rates that is consider reasonable are the amounts prescribed in section 7306 of the Income Tax Regulations. Generally, these rates represent the maximum amount that you can deduct as business expenses.

    Reasonable allowance rates for the use of motor vehicles (per kilometre)

    The rate is $0.07 higher (or $0.04 for 2022 and previous years) in the Yukon, the Northwest Territories and Nunavut.

    What if the reasonable rate is lower or higher

    The type of vehicle and the driving conditions are example of factors that you can use to determine if an allowance should be lower or higher to be considered reasonable in your situation.

    In these situations, you may ask for an advance income tax ruling that is binding on the CRA vis-à-vis the use of a lower or higher reasonable rate. Learn more: Income tax rulings and technical interpretations

    Examples

    Example 1

    You pay an allowance to your employee as follows:

    • a flat per-diem rate to offset the employee's fixed expenses for each day the vehicle is required
    • a reasonable per-kilometre rate for each kilometre driven to offset the operating expenses

    The flat per-diem rate compensates the employee for some of the same use on which the reasonable per-kilometre allowance is based. That is, the fixed expenses incurred by the employee to operate the vehicle.

    The combined amount is considered one allowance and is taxable because it is not based only on the number of kilometres the vehicle is used for employment purposes.

    Example 2

    You pay an allowance to your employee as follows:

    • a flat-rate per month for travel inside the employment district
    • a reasonable per-kilometre rate for employment-related travel outside the employment district

    Since the flat-rate allowance does not cover any of the same use of the vehicle on which the reasonable per-kilometre allowance is based, the allowances are considered separately.

    The reasonable per-kilometre allowance paid for travel outside the district is not taxable. The amount based on a flat-rate paid for travel inside the district is taxable, since it is not based only on the number of kilometres for which the vehicle is used in connection with the employment.

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance provided to your employee for motor vehicle expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable and you must calculate the full amount of the allowances.

    For example, if the employee both received an allowance in respect of their use of the motor vehicle and is reimbursed in whole or in part for expenses in respect of that use (except where the reimbursement is in respect of supplementary business insurance or toll or ferry charges and the amount of the allowance was determined without reference to those reimbursed expenses).

    Examples

    Example 1

    You are a controller at a retirement home. Currently, employees receive a rate of $0.35 per kilometre for automobile and motor vehicle allowances when they have to travel for work to help the residents with their daily tasks. This rate was negotiated in the last collective agreement and the collective agreement is signed for four years. You understands that the $0.35 rate is lower than the reasonable per-kilometre rate.

    In this example, the allowance is considered a taxable benefit because the rate per kilometre is not considered reasonable because it is lower than the reasonable per-kilometre rate.

    Example 2

    You own an office supply company where you employ workers on the road as salespersons. Each salesperson has a specific territory to cover and they use their own vehicle. They are paid a monthly flat rate allowance of $600 no matter where they go or how far they drive. This was agreed on when they were hired and is cited in their respective employment contracts.

    In this example, the flat rate allowance is considered a taxable benefit because it is not based on the number of business kilometers driven. Therefore, the allowance must be included in the employees' income.

    Situation: The employee owns the motor vehicle - You reimburse your employee for motor vehicle expenses

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you reimburse your employee to compensate for the operating costs of a motor vehicle they own, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • It is a reasonable motor vehicle expenses incurred for business purposes  (in connection with or during the course of the office or employment duties)
    • Your employee provides you supporting documents or receipts to confirm the amounts reimbursed
    Example

    An employee uses their automobile approximately 5 times per month to do deliveries for their employer. In exchange for this service, the employer provides the employee with a company credit card to pay for gas for their automobile.

    Based on gas, insurance and wear and tear, the employee estimates that the cost of using their automobile for work totals $250 per year. The company's credit card statements show that the employee spent $1,500 in gas and insurance in the year.

    In this situation, the taxable benefit for the employee is $1,250 ($1,500 minus their cost of $250). The $250 is work-related travel expenses and is not taxable.

    Taxable situation

    If the reimbursement you provide to your employee for motor vehicle expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, it is a taxable benefit.

    Continue to Step 3 - Get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the motor vehicle benefit.

    Situation: The employee owns the motor vehicle - You provide an accountable advance to your employee for motor vehicle expenses

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide an accountable advance to compensate for the operating expenses of a motor vehicle they own, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Your employee accounts with vouchers for their kilometres travelled business purposes  (in connection with or during the course of the office or employment duties) and the amounts they received
    • If your employee was under the amount of the advance, the employee was required to return the difference by the final day of employment for the year or December 31st, whichever is earliest
    • If your employee is over the advance amount, you toped up the advance by the final day of employment for the year or December 31st, whichever is earliest
    • The accountable advance total amount is not more than the total amount for the reasonable allowance rate per-kilometre  
    • You document in your employee’s record the method for establishing and keeping track of the accountable advance
    Examples

    Example 1

    You own a sporting goods store. There is an out of town sales conference your business is participating in and you have scheduled one salesperson to attend. Two weeks prior to travel, you pay an accountable advance to your salesperson to cover expenses relating to the use of their personal vehicle to travel to and from the conference. This amount is based on the reasonable per-kilometre rate. The salesperson accounted for their expenses.

    In this example, the advance is not considered a taxable benefit because:

    • The rate per kilometre is considered reasonable
    • The employee accounted for their expenses by producing vouchers and returned any amount they did not spend

    Example 2

    You are a manager for an insurance company. Your employees travel frequently to meet new and existing clients as part of their duties. Given the frequency of travel of representatives, you use the averaging allowances method to calculate the accountable advance your employees receive each pay to cover eligible expenses relating to the use of their personal vehicles. The amounts are based on the reasonable per-kilometre rate, and are recorded in the employees' files.

    At the end of the year, the employees provide you with a log book that lists the amount of business kilometres they have travelled and any advances they have received. You review the advances paid to your employees and their eligible expenses, at which time the company will pay any amounts owing to the employee or the employee will reimburse any amounts received that exceed their eligible expenses.

    In this example, the advance is not considered a taxable benefit because:

    • there is a pre-established per-kilometre rate that is not more than a reasonable amount
    • the rate and the advance are reasonable under the circumstances
    • the method is documented in the employee's file

    Taxable situation

    If the accountable advance you provide to your employee for motor vehicle expenses do not meet all of the conditions above, it is a taxable benefit.

    Continue to Step 3 - Get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the motor vehicle benefit.

  3. Get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the motor vehicle benefit

    You must get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the motor vehicle benefit. The employee must keep a logbook of the trips made with the motor vehicle and must give you a copy.

    The employee must record the following information:

    • Total number of days that the motor vehicle was made available them during the year
    • Total number of kilometres travelled (on a daily, weekly or monthly basis) during the number of days that the motor vehicle was made available.
    Required records (logbook) of business and personal use

    • Business use information

      The employee must also record, on a daily basis, for each trip made to perform their duties, the following information:

      • Place of departure and the place of destination
      • Number of kilometres travelled between those two places
      • Any other information to establish that the trip is made by the employee to perform their duties

    • Personal use information

      If the automobile is used only for personal purposes , the employee must record the following information:

      • Total number of days that the motor vehicle is made available to the employee (or to a person related to the employee) during the year; and
      • Kilometres registered on the odometer at the beginning and end of the year or of each period during which the motor vehicle is made available, on a continuous basis, to the employee

    • If the employee kept the required records and you received a copy, continue to Step 4 - Determine the calculation to use

    • If the employee did not keep the required records or you did not receive a copy, calculate the full allowance or reimbursement you made, continue to Step 6 - Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST

  4. Determine the calculation to use

    You need to determine if you can use the reduced rate calculation or the reasonable allowances rates calculation to calculate the employee's use of the vehicle between home and work. You can use the reduced rate calculation if all of the following conditions apply:

    • The vehicle is not an automobile
    • You tell your employee in writing that they cannot make any personal use of the vehicle, other than travelling between work and home
    • Your employee kept the required records of business and personal use
    • You have valid business reasons for making the employee take the vehicle home at night, for example:
      • it would not be safe to leave tools and equipment at your premises or at a worksite overnight
      • your employee is on call to respond to emergencies (either to the health and safety of the general population or to a significant disruption to the employer's operations), and you provide the vehicle so the employee can respond more effectively to emergencies
    • The motor vehicle is specifically designed or suited for your business or trade and is essential for the performance of your employee's duties
    Examples

    Example 1

    ABC Restaurant Inc. uses a van that has been specially modified so it can be used more efficiently to transport and deliver food orders. The employer has asked the manager, Leslie, to take the van home at night in case she needs to respond to an emergency at the restaurant, such as an after hour alarm or to fill in on busy nights. Leslie is not allowed to use the van for any other personal use. Since she is the manager, her employment duties do not include delivering food orders, even on a busy night.

    ABC Restaurant cannot use the lower (33 cents per kilometre) rate when calculating Leslie's taxable benefit. These circumstances do not meet all of the conditions of the administrative policy. Although the vehicle has been modified for business needs, Leslie does not need the van to perform her employment duties, so she does not have to bring it home for valid business reasons.

    Example 2

    Mary works for a gas utility company and has to be on-call two weekends out of every month. On these weekends, she has to be able to respond to emergencies directly from her home. The van, which the employer provides Mary for these weekends, displays the company's name, has a light on the roof and has been permanently modified to carry specialized equipment to the scene of the emergencies. The company has a written policy prohibiting any other personal use of these vehicles, other than driving between the worksite and home. Mary has not used the vehicle for any other personal driving.

    Since this situation meets all of the conditions of the administrative policy, Mary's employer may calculate the personal use of the vehicle using the reduced rate of 33 cents per kilometre.

    • If the benefit provided to the employee meets all conditions, continue to Step 5a - Calculate using the reduced rate calculation

    • If the benefit does not meet all conditions, continue to Step 5b - Calculate using the reasonable allowance rate

  5. Calcule the value of the motor vehicle benefit

    You must calculate the benefit related to cost of the employee's personal use  of the vehicle.

    1. Calculate using the reduced rate calculation

      Use the reduced rate calculation ($0,33 fixed rate for 2023 (or $0.29 for 2022) per kilometre for personal use) if all conditions in Step 4 are met to calculate the employee's use of the vehicle.

      Calculations

      • Total number of kilometres travelled for personal purpose
      • multiply by $0.33 for 2023 (fixed rate)
      • equals This is the amount for the operating costs benefit
      • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you for the motor vehicle benefit
      • equals This is the total value of the operating costs benefit
      Calculation example

      In 2022, Matthew was required to use the motor vehicle in connection with his employment in Ontario. The cost of the motor vehicle when it was bought, including options and accessories, and the HST was $50,000. The motor vehicle was made available to Matthew throughout the year (365 days). In verifying his travel, you found that he drove 10,000 km for personal use out of a total of 12,000 km. Matthew reimbursed you $300 for operating expenses 45 days before the end of the year. Matthew meets the conditions to calculate using the fixed rate calculation.

      • 10,000 km is the total number of kilometres Matthew travelled for personal purpose
      • multiply by $0.29 is the fixed rate for 2022
      • equals $2,900 is the amount for the operating costs benefit
      • minus $300 is the amount Matthew reimbursed for the motor vehicle benefit
      • equals $2,600 is the total value of the operating costs benefit
    2. Calculate using the reasonable allowances rates calculation

      Use the reasonable allowance rate  calculation if the conditions in Step 4 are not met to calculate the employee's use of the vehicle.

      Calculations

      • Total number of kilometres travelled for personal purpose
      • multiply by Reasonable allowance rate - First 5,000 kilometres
      • multiply by Reasonable allowance rate - Additional kilometres
      • equals This is the amount for the operating costs benefit
      • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you for the operating costs benefit
      • equals This is the total value of the operating costs benefit
      Calculation example

      In 2022, Matthew was required to use the motor vehicle in connection with his employment in Ontario. The cost of the motor vehicle when it was bought, including options and accessories, and the HST was $50,000. The automobile was made available to Matthew throughout the year (365 days). In verifying his travel, you found that he drove 10,000 km for personal use out of a total of 12,000 km. Matthew reimbursed you $300 for operating expenses 45 days before the end of the year. Matthew does not meet the conditions to calculate using the fixed rate calculation.

      • 10,000 km is the total number of kilometres Matthew travelled for personal purpose
      • multiply by $0.61 is the fixed rate for 2022 on the first 5,000 kilometres
      • multiply by $0.55 is the fixed rate for 2022 on the additional kilometres
      • equals $5,800 ($3,050 + $2,750) is the amount for the operating costs benefit
      • minus $300 is the amount Matthew reimbursed for the operating costs benefit
      • equals $5,500 is the total value of the operating costs benefit

      Employee's allowable employment expenses

      Your employee may be able to claim certain employment expenses on their income tax and benefit return if, under the contract of employment, the employee had to pay for their expenses. This contract of employment does not have to be in writing but you and your employee have to agree to the terms and understand what is expected.

      In this situation, you need to complete Form Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment and give it to your employee so they can deduct employment expenses from their income. By signing the form, you are only certifying that the employee met the conditions of employment and had to pay for the expenses under their employment contract.

      It is the employee's responsibility to claim the expenses on their income tax and benefits return and to keep records to support the claim.

      Examples

      Example 1

      You allow your employees to use their personal motor vehicle for business and pay them a monthly motor vehicle allowance to pay for the operating expenses and you include the allowance in the employees' employment income as a taxable benefit

      Example 2

      You have a formal telework arrangement with your employees that allows the employees to work at home. Your employees pays for the expenses of their work space on their own

  6. Withhold payroll deductions and remit GST/HST

    If the benefit is taxable, you must withhold the following deductions. The amounts must be included in the pay period they were received or enjoyed.

    The withholding and remitting requirement depends on the type of remuneration: cash , non-cash , or near-cash .

    • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

      Withhold:

      • Income tax
      • CPP
      • EI (do not withhold)

      Remit:

      • GST/HST

    • Cash: Option 2

      Withhold:

      • Income tax
      • CPP
      • EI

      Remit:

      • GST/HST - Reimbursements
      • GST/HST - Allowances (do not remit)

    Learn more on how to calculate deductions and the GST/HST to remit on benefits: How to calculate - Calculate payroll deductions and contributions

  7. Report the benefit on a slip

    If the benefit is taxable, you must report the following on the T4 slip.

    • Non-cash and near-cash: Option 1

      Report on:

      • Box 14 - Employment Income
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
      • Code 40 - Other Information

    • Cash: Option 2

      Report on:

      • Box 14 - Employment Income
      • Box 24 - EI insurable earnings
      • Box 26 - CPP/QPP pensionable earnings
      • Code 40 - Other Information
