Security (stock) options

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may grant stock options to your employees as a form of compensation to encourage their greater involvement in the corporation. Generally, there are no tax consequences to the employee at the time the option is granted. There is no obligation for the employee to exercise the stock option.

Determine if the benefit was received by virtue of employment

You need to determine if there is an agreement between a qualifying person (corporation or a mutual trust fund) and the employee.

There may be a written stock option agreement, or if there is no written agreement, the terms for issuing securities, stock and trust units in the financial records of a corporation (for example, a directors' resolution in the corporate minutes or notes to financial statements) will determine the employment status of the person receiving the benefit.

Agreement between a qualifying person and the person ceased to be an employee [7(4)]

By virtue of employment

If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and the person ceased to be an employee, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment.

Explanation

If a person was an employee at the time of receiving a right to acquire shares, but subsequently ceases to be an employee (for example, change jobs or retire) before the exercise or transfer of that right he is treated as an employee until the transaction is completed. The benefit is included in his employment income and he is deemed to have received the benefit as if he were still employed.

Example
  • A person had the option to purchase 10,000 shares of ABC Inc. when the employment began
  • On November 30, 2020, the person employment ceased when they resigned from his position as Manager
  • A clause in the stock option agreement allowed the person to purchase these shares within a period of 90 calendar days after the date of termination
  • On January 20, 2021, the person chose to purchase 20,000 shares at the price of $.50 cents per share

Although the person's employment ceased on November 30, 2020, any benefit from the purchase of shares in ABC Inc. on January 20, 2021 is considered taxable under subsection 7(1)

Agreement between a qualifying person and an employee who is a shareholder [7(5)]

Not by virtue of employment

If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person who is a shareholder, the benefit is not considered received by virtue of employment if all of the following apply:

  • Benefit is a distribution of profits
  • Similar benefits are not available to employees who were not shareholders
  • Duties of other employees not equally important
Explanation

Under 7(5), section 7 does not apply if "the benefit conferred by the agreement was not received in respect of, in the course of, or by virtue of, the employment".

If the employee is also a shareholder, it is a question of fact whether the individual received the shares in their capacity as a shareholder or an employee. Section 7 does not apply when the benefit conferred by the stock option agreement was not received by reason of the individual's employment.

If an individuals' or a group of individuals' shareholding are sufficiently large so as to enable them to be in a position to control the company's actions, it is questionable whether the benefit was received "by virtue of employment".

You need to determine if the benefit resulted from the individual's role as an employee or their role as a shareholder. In addition to control, other factors must be considered.

Example
  • Person is president of ABC Inc.
  • Owns 51% of that corporation's common voting shares
  • Spouse owns 10%
  • Person and his spouse are the only people with a stock option agreement from ABC Inc.
  • The plan was not offered to any other employee.

The person is in a position of control. The person and the spouse receive the benefit because of his capacity as a shareholder. Section 7 of the ITA does not apply because the benefit was not received "in respect of", "in the course of", or "by virtue of", employment.

By virtue of employment

If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person who is not a shareholder or does not meet all of the above conditions, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment.

Agreement between a qualifying person and a self-employed person [7(5)]

Not by virtue of employment

If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and a self-employed person, this is not considered received by virtue of employment.

Explanation

This is not taxable as an employee benefit because it is a self-employed person and it is not considered received by virtue of employment. Under 7(5), the section 7 does not apply if "the benefit conferred by the agreement was not received in respect of, in the course of, or by virtue of, the employment".

Example

A person is a self-employed consultant. The person is contracted on a short-term basis by a junior public mining corporation to raise capital. Part of the contract, the person was offered participation in the corporation's stock option benefit plan with details outlined in a stock option agreement.

The person was not an employee of the junior public mining corporation. Section 7 does not apply because the benefit was not received "in respect of", "in the course of", or "by virtue of", employment.

Agreement between a qualifying person and a non-resident employer

By virtue of employment

If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and a non-resident employer, the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment.

Explanation

The wording of section 7 of the ITA is broad enough to encompass where a non-resident corporation (whether public or private) agrees to sell or issue shares (either of itself or of another corporation with which it does not deal at arm's length) to individuals either:

  • Employed by it in Canada
  • Employed in Canada by a corporation with which the non-resident corporation does not deal at arm's length

For example, where the foreign parent of a Canadian subsidiary agrees to sell or issue shares of the parent corporation to employees of the subsidiary.

Care must be taken in reviewing these cases to ensure that the foreign plan conforms to Canadian tax rules in order to determine if sections 7 and 110(1)(d) apply. (For example, the shares concerned may not meet the requirements under regulation 6204(1) to be considered prescribed shares. Consequently, the employee would not be eligible for the security options deduction under 110(1)(d).)

When calculating the taxable benefit, the foreign stock options must be converted to the Canadian dollar equivalent. Use the exchange rate (available from the company or the Bank of Canada) in effect on the dates that make up the transaction (acquisition date): the date the shares are paid for and the date when the treasury shares have been transferred to the employee. Where the amount paid or to be paid by an employee pursuant to the terms of a stock option agreement is expressed in terms of a foreign currency, it will not compromise the employee's eligibility for the deduction under paragraph 110(1)(d).

Example
  • On March 7, an employee is granted 100 options to purchase shares of their employer's parent corporation at the FMV of $10 US ($1 US = $1.35 CDN).
  • On September 2 the employee informs their employer that they wish to exercise their 100 options and pays the employer $1000 US ($1 US = $1.25 CDN). The exercise price is $10 US and the FMV is $20 US on this date.
  • On September 5 the treasury shares are transferred to the employee at a FMV of $19 US ($1 US = $1.20 CDN).
Agreement between a qualifying person and a trustee (as an intermediary)

By virtue of employment

If the stock option agreement is between a qualifying person and a trustee (as an intermediary), the benefit is considered received by virtue of employment.

Explanation

When the employer uses a trust as an intermediary to sell or issue its shares held in a corporation with which it does not deal at arm's length, the employee is still considered to be dealing with the employer per section 7(2) of the ITA. Where the trustee holds the securities for an employee, acquisitions and dispositions of the securities by the trustee are treated as acquisitions and dispositions by the employee for the purposes of section 7 and paragraphs 110(1)(d), 110(1)(d.1) and (d.01) of the Act.

Where the employee exercises a security option and the agreement provides for the shares to be held in trust, but the employee does not meet the conditions of the agreement and the shares are forfeited back to the issuing corporation, there is no change to the taxable benefit reported on the T4. However, the employee may become eligible for a deduction under subsection 8(12) of the Income Tax Act. When the individual is eligible, this deduction may be claimed when filing the T1, and is equal to the value of the stock option benefit previously received (less 50% if the individual has previously claimed the stock option deduction for the benefit).

Example

Ashley is granted 10,000 options on March 4, 2014, when the FMV of each share is $20. Ashley must be an employee for at least three years after date of exercise before the shares are fully vested, or the employer may buy back the shares for $0.01 each. Ashley exercises her option on May 1, 2015, when the FMV of each share is $25. In 2015, Ashley's benefit is equal to $50,000 [10,000 x (25-20)], and this amount is reported on her T4.

On June 1, 2016, Ashley leaves the company, and as a result, forfeits her securities, and receives $100 in consideration for the $50,000 of shares. There are no changes to Ashley's T4 for 2014, 2015, or 2016, but when Ashley files her tax return, she may, in the year in which she forfeited the securities, claim a deduction of $50,000 from her income.

No stock option agreement

By virtue of employment

If there is no stock option agreement, the amount paid to the employee is considered received by virtue of employment.

Determine when the benefit is taxable based on the event and if eligible to claim a deduction

The benefit is taxable when one of the following applies:

  • Employee acquires, purchases or receives the securities at less than the FMV
  • Employee sells, transfers, or disposes of options rights for a financial gain

Whether the employee will be eligible for the security options deduction for the benefit depends on the event that cause the benefit:

Events that cause the employee benefit

  • Employee chooses to exercises their rights and acquires the shares [7(1)(a)]
  • Employee transfers or disposes of rights to a person with whom the employee was dealing at arm's length [7(1)(b)] or to the particular qualifying person with whom the employee was not dealing at arm's length [7(1)(b.1)], including security option cash outs
  • Employee transfers rights to a non-arm's length person, and that person exercises the option to acquire the shares [7(1)(c)]
  • Employee transfers rights to a non-arm's length person, and that person disposes of the rights [7(1)(d)] and [7(1)(d.1)]
  • Employee dies holding unexercised options [7(1)(e)]
  • Employee exchanges shares acquired from a CCPC under the security option agreement for new shares [7(1.5)]
  • Employee is no longer able to exercise their rights under the security option agreement [7(1.7)]
  • Phantom stock plans (or stock appreciation rights)

7(1.5)

Employee exchanges shares acquired from a CCPC under the security option agreement for new shares [7(1.5)]

If an employee exchanges shares on a share-for-share basis or as part of a corporate reorganization or capital restructuring within a non-arm’s length corporate group, an employee exchanges shares acquired under the security option agreement for new shares, where certain conditions are met, the benefit is not taxable.

Steps

  1. Determine if the benefit taxable

    Non-taxable situation

    If your employee exchanges the shares of a CCPC for new shares, the benefit from the transaction is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Employee exchanges their shares for new shares of the employer corporation, a corporation with which the employer corporation does not deal at arm's length immediately after the disposition or exchange of the old shares, the newly amalgamated or merged corporation, or a corporation with which the newly amalgamated or merged corporation does not deal at arm's length immediately after the amalgamation
    • Employee receives no consideration for the exchange other than the new option
    • Fair market value of the new shares immediately after the disposition or exchange is not more than the fair market value of the old shares immediately before the disposition or exchange

    The employee is deemed under 7(1.5) to have not disposed of the old shares and the new shares are deemed to be the same as and a continuation of the old option.

    Taxable situation

    If the transaction does not meet all of the above, the benefit is taxable to the employee when the new option has been exercised or otherwise disposed of by the employee.

    The benefit is taxable to the employee in the taxation year in which the old shares are disposed of under 7(1.1).

    • If the benefit is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations. Do not continue to next step.

    • Employee acquired the shares after May 22, 1985
    • Employee dealt at arm's length with the corporation or any other corporation involved right after the agreement was concluded
    • CCPC shares disposed of in the year where the employee dealt at arm's length with the corporation
    • Employee has not disposed of the shares (otherwise than as a result of the employee's death) or exchanged them within two years after the date the taxpayer acquired them
    • Employee did not deduct an amount under 110(1)(d) of the ITA for the benefit

7(1.7)

Employee is no longer able to exercise their rights under the security option agreement [7(1.7)]

Where your employee had rights to acquire shares under a security options agreement and the rights cease to be exercisable under the agreement, your employee may be deemed to have received a taxable benefit depending on the consideration they had received for the option ceasing to be exercisable.

Examples of this situation includes merger where the previous corporation no longer exists for the employee to exercise the option and situations where the option is cancelled by the employer.

Steps

  1. Determine if the benefit taxable

    Situations

    Situation: Your employee has exchanged their rights for rights under a new stock plan

    If the employee has exchanged their rights for rights under a new stock plan, continue to: Employee transfers or disposes of rights to a person with whom the employee was dealing at arm’s length [7(1)(b)] or to the particular qualifying person with whom the employee was not dealing at arm’s length [7(1)(b.1)], including security option cash outs.

    Situation: Your employee is no longer able to exercise their rights under the option agreement and received consideration for the loss of their ability to exercise the option

    Taxable situation

    If the employee is no longer able to exercise their rights under the security option agreement and receives consideration for this loss, the employee is considered to have disposed of the rights.

    The benefit is taxable to the employee in the taxation year in which the rights are disposed of.

    Situation: Your employee is no longer able to exercise their rights under the option agreement and did not receive consideration for the loss of the ability to exercise the option

    Non-taxable situation

    If the employee is no longer able to exercise their rights under the security option agreement and does not receive consideration for this loss, while the employee is considered to have disposed of the rights, as they have not received any consideration, the benefit is not taxable to the employee.

    • If the benefit is not taxable, you do not need to do any calculations. Do not continue to next step.
    • If the benefit is taxable, continue to Step 2 - Determine if the employee is eligible for the option benefit deduction – Paragraph 110(1)(d).

  2. Determine if the employee is eligible for the option benefit deduction – Paragraph 110(1)(d)

    Where your employee cashes out the rights under the security option agreement, they are eligible to claim the option benefit deduction under paragraph 110(1)(d) if all of the following apply:

    • The security is a prescribed share (common shares, per 6204(1) of the employer corporation's capital stock, or those of a non-arm's length corporation) or a unit of a mutual fund trust
    • The employee dealt at arm's length with the qualifying person (corporation or mutual fund trust) right after the agreement was made
    • At the time when the agreement was made, the price of the share or unit was not less than its FMV

    If your employee:

    • Meets all conditions, the employee is eligible for the option benefit deduction under paragraph 110(1)(d). Continue to Step 3 - Determine if the employee is subject to a $200,000 vesting limit on the option benefit deduction – Paragraph 110(1)(d).
    • Does not meet all conditions, the employee is not eligible for the on the option benefit deduction – Paragraph 110(1)(d). Continue to Step 4 - You want to calculate and the employee is not eligible to any deductions.

  3. Determine if the employee is subject to a $200,000 vesting limit on the option benefit deduction – Paragraph 110(1)(d)

    For security options granted on or after July 1, 2021 (other than options granted after June 2021 that replace options granted before July 2021), the employee is subject to a $200,000 annual vesting limit under paragraph 110(1)(d) if the qualifying person meets both of the following conditions:

    • Is not a Canadian-controlled private corporation (CCPC)
    • Has, or is part of a consolidated group that has, gross revenues of more than $500 million

    The employee is not subject to a $200,000 vesting limit on the option benefit under 110(1)(d) if one of the following apply:

    • It is a CCPC
    • It is a non-CCPC with gross revenues of less than $500 million
    • For options granted before July 1, 2021, it is a non-CCPC with revenues of $500 million or more
    • For options granted after July 1, 2021 replacing options granted before July 2021, it is a non-CCPC with revenues of $500 million or more

    If your employee:

    • Meets all conditions, the employee is not subject to a $200,000 vesting limit on the option benefit under 110(1)(d). Continue to Step 4 - You want to calculate and the employee is eligible under 110(1)(d).
    • Does not meet all conditions, the employee is subject to a $200,000 vesting limit under paragraph 110(1)(d). Continue to Step 4 - You want to calculate and the employee is eligible under 110(1)(d) and subject to a $200,000 vesting limit.

Phantom stock plans

Phantom stock plans (or stock appreciation rights)

Phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan is an arrangement where the employee is compensated based on how the shares of the employer corporation perform on the open market.

Taxable situation

Phantom stock plan or stock appreciation plan received because of employment is taxable to the employee on the date that the cash was received.

Elibibility for the option benefit deduction

Your employee is not eligible to claim the option benefit deduction because they received cash based on the share value increasing.

This is because there is never any intention in the agreement for the shares to be issued to the employee.

Calculate the value of the benefit

Situation: You want to calculate the benefit on amount paid to the employee (received cash based on share value)

The amount must be included in the pay period were the cash was received and the value of the benefit is equal to the amount paid to your employee under the stock plan:

  • Fair market value of total shares at the time of the payment
  • minus Fair market value of total shares at the time the plan began
  • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
  • equals Value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip
Example 1 - Calculations

An employee is offered rights under a plan where they will be compensated in cash for the performance of 100 shares of their employer on the stock market. As part of the plan, the employee did not have the option to acquire the shares. At the time the plan began, the shares had a FMV of $40 per share. At the time of the payment, the shares had a FMV of $55 per share.

  • $5,500 is the fair market value of total shares at the time of the payment
  • minus $4,000 is the fair market value of total shares at the time the plan began
  • minus $0 because the employee does not reimburse the employer
