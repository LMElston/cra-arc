Automobile or motor vehicle benefits – Allowances or reimbursements provided to an employee for the use of their own vehicle

Content has been updated for clarity, completeness and plain language. No changes were made to the current CRA administrative policy.

You may provide an allowance, an advance or reimburse your employee to compensate for use of their vehicle in connection with or in the course of their office or employment.

  • Employer
    Includes a person related to the employer
  • Employee and shareholder
    Includes a person related to the employee or shareholder
  • Steps
  • References

Steps

  1. Determine if the employee is required to use the automobile or motor vehicle in connection with or in the course of an office or employment

    You need to determine if the employee was required to use the automobile or motor vehicle in connection with or in the course of an office or employment.

    • If you did not require your employee to use the vehicle as part of the duties of an office or employment, the benefit is taxable.

      Continue to Step 5 - Calculate the value of the automobile or motor vehicle benefit to be included on the T4 slip.

    • If you required your employee to use the vehicle as part of the duties of an office or employment, the benefit may not be taxable if they meet all conditions.

      Continue to Step 2 - Get the required records from your employee.

  2. Get the required records from your employee

    To meet the condition under the Income Tax Act, you must get the required records from your employee to accurately calculate the vehicle benefit. Based on these records, you need to calculate the value of the benefit based on the number of kilometres driven in the course of their employment.

    • If you do not receive the required records, the benefit is taxable.

      Continue to Step 5 - Calculate the value of the automobile or motor vehicle benefit to be included on the T4 slip.

    • If your employee kept the required detailed records and you received a copy, the benefit meets the condition of the ITA, the benefit may not be taxable.

      Continue to Step 3 - Determine if special considerations apply to the automobile or motor vehicle benefit.

  3. Determine if special considerations apply to the automobile or motor vehicle benefit

    The benefit may not be taxable if the benefit paid to your employee for the use of their vehicle in connection with or in the course of their office or employment is done in one of the following special situations:

    • Refer to: Your employee is working in a prescribed zone
    • Refer to: Your employee has a severe or prolonged impairment
    • Refer to: Your employee works in a remote location
    • Refer to: Your employee works at a special work site
    • If the use of the vehicle is in one of the above special situation, do not continue to next step.
    • If the use of the vehicle is not in one of the above special situations, the benefit paid for the use of their vehicle in connection with or in the course of their office or employment may be a taxable benefit. Continue to Step 4 - Determine if the benefit is taxable.

  4. Determine if the benefit is taxable

    Depending on your situation, if you pay amounts to your employee for a vehicle they own and use in connection with or in the course of their office or employment, the benefit may not be taxable.

    Situations

    Situation: You provide an allowance to your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the Income Tax Act (ITA) exemption, if you paid an allowance to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle in connection with or in the course of their office or employment, the benefit is not taxable and your employee does not have to provide you with supporting documents or receipts, if all of the following apply:

    • The allowance is calculated only on the actual number of kilometres for business purposes  (in connection with or during the course of the office or employment duties)
    • The rate per-kilometre is reasonable , meaning:
      • The rate matches the reasonable allowance rate per-kilometre
      • If the rate does not match the reasonable allowance rate per-kilometre, you must be able to justify your position
    • You do not reimburse the employee for the expenses related to the same use of the vehicle, unless the reimbursement is for supplementary business insurance, or tolls and ferry charges not covered by the allowance
    Reasonable allowance rates for the use of motor vehicles (per kilometre)

    What if the reasonable rates are different from the prescribed rate

    The type of vehicle and the driving conditions are example of factors that you can use to determine if an allowance should be lower or higher to be considered reasonable in your situation.

    In these situations, you may ask for an advance income tax ruling that is binding on the CRA vis-à-vis the use of a lower or higher reasonable rate. Learn more: Income tax rulings and technical interpretations

    Examples

    Example 1

    You pay an allowance to your employee as follows:

    • A flat per-diem rate to offset the employee's fixed expenses for each day the vehicle is required
    • A reasonable per-kilometre rate for each kilometre driven to offset the operating expenses

    The flat per-diem rate compensates the employee for some of the same use on which the reasonable per-kilometre allowance is based. That is, the fixed expenses incurred by the employee to operate the vehicle.

    The combined amount is considered one allowance and is taxable because it is not based only on the number of kilometres the vehicle is used for employment purposes.

    Example 2

    You pay an allowance to your employee as follows:

    • A flat-rate per month for travel inside the employment district
    • A reasonable per-kilometre rate for employment-related travel outside the employment district

    Since the flat-rate allowance does not cover any of the same use of the vehicle on which the reasonable per-kilometre allowance is based, the allowances are considered separately.

    The reasonable per-kilometre allowance paid for travel outside the district is not taxable. The amount based on a flat-rate paid for travel inside the district is taxable, since it is not based only on the number of kilometres for which the vehicle is used in connection with the employment.

    Taxable situation

    If the allowance provided to your employee for motor vehicle expenses does not meet all of the conditions above, the benefit is taxable.

    Automobile and motor vehicles allowances which are taxable and must be included in the employee's income include:

    • Allowances paid at per-kilometre rates that are not considered reasonable because they are either too high or too low
    • Flat-rate allowances paid to employees where the amount paid is not related to the number of kilometres driven
    • Allowances that combine flat-rate and reasonable per-kilometre allowances for the same use of the vehicle
    • Allowances received where the employee is also reimbursed for the same use of the vehicle (except where the reimbursement is in respect of supplementary business insurance or toll or ferry charges and the amount of the allowance was determined without reference to those reimbursed expenses)
    Examples

    Example 1

    You are a controller at a retirement home. Currently, employees receive a rate of $0.35 per kilometre for automobile and motor vehicle allowances when they have to travel for work to help the residents with their daily tasks. This rate was negotiated in the last collective agreement and the collective agreement is signed for four years. You understand that the $0.35 rate is lower than the reasonable per-kilometre rate.

    In this example, the allowance is considered a taxable benefit because the rate per kilometre is not considered reasonable because it is lower than the reasonable per-kilometre rate. Therefore, the allowance must to be included in the employees' income, and CPP, EI, and income tax must be deducted from the allowance.

    It is important to note that collective agreements do not supersede the Income Tax Act.

    If the allowance does not cover all of the employee’s out-of-pocket expenses, they would be eligible to claim their auto and motor vehicle expenses on their income tax return. To do so, you as the employer will need to complete Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment. For more information, see Employees' allowable employment expenses.

    Example 2

    You own an office supply company where you employ workers on the road as salespersons. Each salesperson has a specific territory to cover and they use their own vehicle. They are paid a monthly flat rate allowance of $600 no matter where they go or how far they drive. This was agreed on when they were hired and is cited in their respective employment contracts.

    In this example, the flat rate allowance is considered a taxable benefit because it is not based on the number of business kilometres driven. Therefore, the allowance must be included in the employees' income.

    Situation: You reimburse your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you reimburse your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle in connection with or in the course of their office or employment, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • It is a reasonable reimbursement of automobile or motor vehicle expenses incurred for business purposes  (in connection with or during the course of the office or employment duties)
    • Your employee provides you supporting documents or receipts to confirm the amounts reimbursed
    Example

    An employee uses their automobile approximately 5 times per month to do deliveries for their employer. In exchange for this service, the employer provides the employee with a company credit card to pay for gas for their automobile.

    Based on gas, insurance and wear and tear, the employee estimates that the cost of using their automobile for work totals $250 per year. The company's credit card statements show that the employee spent $1,500 in gas and insurance in the year.

    In this situation, the taxable benefit for the employee is $1,250 ($1,500 minus their cost of $250). The $250 is work-related travel expenses and is not taxable.

    Taxable situation

    If the reimbursement you provide to your employee for automobile or motor vehicle expenses does not meet all of the conditions above, it is a taxable benefit.

    Situation: You provide an accountable advance to your employee

    Non-taxable situation

    Under the CRA's administrative policy, if you provide an accountable advance to your employee to compensate for using their own vehicle in connection with or in the course of their office or employment, the benefit is not taxable if all of the following apply:

    • Your employee provides you with vouchers to account for their kilometres travelled for business purposes  (in connection with or during the course of the office or employment duties)
    • There is a pre-established per-kilometre rate that is not more than a reasonable amount
    • The rate and the advances are reasonable under the circumstances
    • You document in your employee’s record the method for establishing and keeping track of the accountable advance
    • No other provision of the Income Tax Act requires you to include the advances in the employee’s income
    • If your employee’s expenses were under the amount of the advance, the employee was required to return the difference by the final day of employment for the year or December 31st, whichever is earliest
    • If your employee’s expenses were over the advance amount, you topped up the advance by the final day of employment for the year or December 31st, whichever is earliest

    You are not allowed to take a flat-rate or lump-sum allowance not based on the amount of kilometres driven and average it at the end of the year to determine a reasonable per-kilometre rate and then be excluded from the employee’s income.

    Examples

    Example 1

    You own a sporting goods store. There is an out of town sales conference your business is participating in and you have scheduled one salesperson to attend. Two weeks prior to travel, you pay an accountable advance to your salesperson to cover expenses relating to the use of their personal vehicle to travel to and from the conference. This amount is based on the reasonable per-kilometre rate. The salesperson accounted for their expenses.

    In this example, the advance is not considered a taxable benefit because:

    • The rate per kilometre is considered reasonable
    • The employee accounted for their expenses by producing vouchers and returned any amount they did not spend

    Example 2

    You are a manager for an insurance company. Your employees travel frequently to meet new and existing clients as part of their duties. Given the frequency of travel of representatives, you use the averaging allowances method to calculate the accountable advance your employees receive each pay to cover eligible expenses relating to the use of their personal vehicles. The amounts are based on the reasonable per-kilometre rate, and are recorded in the employees' files.

    At the end of the year, the employees provide you with a log book that lists the amount of business kilometres they have travelled and any advances they have received. You review the advances paid to your employees and their eligible expenses, at which time the company will pay any amounts owing to the employee or the employee will reimburse any amounts received that exceed their eligible expenses.

    In this example, the advance is not considered a taxable benefit because:

    • There is a pre-established per-kilometre rate that is not more than a reasonable amount
    • The rate and the advance are reasonable under the circumstances
    • The method is documented in the employee's file

    Taxable situation

    If the accountable advance you provide to your employee for motor vehicle expenses does not meet all of the conditions above, it is a taxable benefit.

    Situation: Self-employed individual or an individual who is a member of a partnership owns or leases a motor vehicle

    The information on this webpage does not apply to a self-employed individual or an individual who is a member of a partnership who personally owns or leases a motor vehicle to earn income from the business.

  5. Calculate the value of the automobile or motor vehicle benefit to be included on the T4 slip

    If the benefit is taxable, the value of the benefit is equal to:

    • Value of the benefit received or enjoyed
    • minus Any amounts your employee reimbursed you
    • equals Value of the benefit to be included on the T4 slip

    Continue to Step 6 - Determine if you need to fill out Form T2200 if the benefit is taxable.

  6. Determine if you need to fill out Form T2200 if the benefit is taxable

    You may need to fill out Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment, if your employee is eligible to deduct part of the cost.

    If you include an allowance as income for your employee and the allowance is considered to low, you must fill out and sign Form T2200, Declaration of Conditions of Employment and your employee can deduct:

    • An amount up to the amount of the allowance (if the employee did not keep a record or does not wish to claim employment expenses)
    • Expenses paid in the year (if a record was kept)
    • No expense if the allowance is not added to his or her income

    By signing the form, you are only certifying that the employee met the conditions of employment and had to pay for the expenses under their employment contract.

    It is the employee's responsibility to claim the expenses on their income tax and benefits return and to keep records to support the claim.

    Examples

    Example 1

    You allow your employees to use their personal motor vehicle for business and pay them a monthly motor vehicle allowance to pay for the operating expenses and you include the allowance in the employees' employment income as a taxable benefit.

    Example 2

    You have a formal telework arrangement with your employees that allows the employees to work at home. Your employees pays for the expenses of their work space on their own.
