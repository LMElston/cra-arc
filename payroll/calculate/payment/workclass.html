Guidance on the worker classification: Employee or self-employed

Content has been developed to provide guidance only and it does not replace the law. No changes were made to the existing legislative requirement.

Why do you need to determine the employment status

It is important to decide whether a worker is an employee or a self-employed individual. Employment status directly affects a person's entitlement to employment insurance (EI) benefits under the Employment Insurance Act. It can also have an impact on how a worker is treated under other legislation such as the Canada Pension Plan and the Income Tax Act.

The facts of the working relationship as a whole decide the employment status.

What is considered an employee or a self-employed (independent contractor)
Employee
A person who works in the service of another person (the employer) under an express or implied contract of hire, under which the employer has the right to control the details of work performance.
Self-employed worker (independent contractor)
One who is hired to undertake a specific project but who is left free to do the assigned work and to choose the method for accomplishing it. Unlike an employee, an independent contractor who commits a wrong while carrying out the work does not create liability for the one who did the hiring.

Learn more about CPP, EI and income tax deductions: Get ready to make deductions

Who can use the guidance to determine employment status

Employers

If you are an employer, use this guidance if:

  • You are directly hiring, or have directly hired, a worker
  • You are in receipt of a worker's services who operates through their own intermediary
  • There has been a change to an existing contract or service agreement
Payers of other amounts

If you are payer of other amounts, use this guidance if you want to:

  • Check the accuracy of a determination you have been given
  • Use the determination as part of a status disagreement process
Workers

If you are a worker, use this guidance if:

  • You want to check the accuracy of a determination you have been given
  • You want to use the determination as part of a status disagreement process
  • You are providing your services through an intermediary to a small-sized non-public sector client and need to make a determination as part of your responsibilities

You may also want to use the guidance if any of this criteria applies and there has been a change to an existing contract or service agreement.

Before you start

Before you start, you need to know:

  • Details of the contract
  • The worker's responsibilities
  • Who decides what work needs to be done
  • Who decides when, where and how the work is done
  • How the worker will be paid
  • If the engagement includes any corporate benefits or reimbursement for expenses

Factors to consider

You need to consider multiple factors to determine if the worker is an employee or self-employed.

  1. Determine if the type of employment is considered a special situation

    Before you use this guidance, you need to determine if the type of employment is considered a special situation, this includes:

    • Barbers and hairdressers
    • Caregivers, baby-sitters and domestic workers
    • Fishers
    • Circus, fair, parade or similar activity

    Learn more: Determine the tax treatment of payments other than regular employment income

    • Do not continue to next step if the type of employment is in a special situation.
    • If no special situation apply, continue to Step 2 - Contract arrangement.

  2. Contract arrangement

    The CRA asks the worker and the payer what their intent was when they entered into the working arrangement. Workers and payers can choose how they set up their affairs. However, the employment status they choose must reflect their working relationship.

    What is considered a valid contract

    Validity of the contract

    Contracts can be formed either orally or in writing. An oral contract is as valid and enforceable as a written contract.

    Definition of the essential details of a contract

    There must be compensation or a reward for a party to carry out its obligations under the contract. Consideration
    The parties involved must be legally capable of entering into the contract. Capacity
    There must be an offer and an acceptance of the terms and conditions of the agreement from the parties. Offer and acceptance
    The object of the contract must be legitimate. Obligation/Lawful purpose
    The agreement must be mutual, i.e., the parties must have the intention to enter into the contract and be bound by its terms. Consent/Intent to be legally bound

    Who decides when a contract is null and void

    When addressing the issue of statutory illegality, the courts can decide that a contract is null and void from the beginning (void ab initio) if the formation of the contract was prohibited by statute and if, in the court's assessment, it would not be contrary to public policy to declare the contract void ab initio.

    Cessation of a contract

    The conditions that can terminate a contract are as follows:

    • The death of one of the parties.
    • The expiry of the time stipulated for the duration of the contract by one of the following:
      • the parties
      • law, in the absence of any by the parties
    • "Confusion" arising as a result of both parties to the contract becoming one in the same (for example: an individual buys a business (sole proprietorship) that they have an existing contract with and becomes both parties to the same contract)
    • Misrepresentation
    • Fraud
    • Duress
    • The effect of a resolutory clause in the contract. This would be a clause in the contract that serves to release the parties to the contract from an obligation in the event of a stipulated occurrence or series of events.
    • The disrespect of the main clauses of the contract, or significant breach of the conditions outlined in the contract.
    • A judgment from a competent court.
    • When the performance required by the contract becomes impossible for one or more of the parties to the contract to honour. Reasons such as a change in law making it illegal for one of the parties to meet their obligations, or the physical incapacity (injury or mental illness) of one or more of the parties can cause a contract to become impossible to honour.
    • When both the obligations and the consideration set out in the contract have been satisfied. In other words, once a contract is completed, it is at its cessation.
    Examples of questions the CRA may ask you to determine the status

    Validity of a contract

    • Are the parties to the contract of legal age (as per the respective provincial Employment Standards site)? If the worker is not of legal age, have the parents or legal guardians provided written consent for the minor child to work?
    • Did the parties have any mental disabilities that would prevent them from understanding the nature of the contract?
    • What was the offer, which is, what was the worker hired to do?
    • What did the worker agree to do?
    • Where was the work to be performed?
    • What was the agreed consideration for providing the service?
    • Was there an agreed method of payments?
    • Was there an agreed duration for the service to be performed?
    • Is the party a citizen or permanent resident of Canada? If no, does the party have a work permit to legally work in Canada?

    Intent of the parties

    • When you first entered into the agreement, what was your understanding of the work relationship (employee or self-employed)?
    • Why do you feel that you were an employee or a self-employed person?
    • Was there a written contract? Was the contract signed by both parties?
    • Were there any amendments to the original agreement? If yes, was it verbal or written? You may need to provide details of the verbal amendments or you may need to provide a copy of the amendments.

    Documents to review

    • Payer's payroll records - Is the worker listed on payer's payroll?
    • Were there CPP, EI or income tax deductions withheld from the worker's pay?
    • Was a pay stub given to the worker when he/she was remunerated?
    • Was the worker issued a T4 information slip?
    • In addition to remuneration, did the worker receive vacation pay, paid vacation leave, statutory holiday or sick leave?
    • Did the worker participate or belong to the payer's private pension plan?
    • Is the worker entitled or belonged to the payer's benefit plans, for example: fitness membership, medical or dental plans?
    • Is the worker listed as part of the staff in the payer's staff phone directory or payer's website?
    • Is the worker named as part of the staff in any of the payer's business brochure?
    • Did the payer provide business cards to the worker? If yes, did the business cards bear the payer's business name? What was the worker's title or position listed on the business cards?

    • Employer-employee

      The two parties intend to enter into a contract of service (COS)

    • Self-employed (indipendent contractor)

      The two parties intend to enter into a contract for services

      (CFS)

  3. Control

    Control is the ability, authority, or right of a payer to exercise control over a worker concerning the manner in which the work is done and what work will be done.

    Degree of control or independence

    Consider the degree of control held by the payer or the degree of independence held by the worker.

    The actual degree of control will vary with the type of work and the skills of the worker.

    Deciding the degree of control can be difficult when examining the employment of professionals such as engineers, doctors, and IT consultants. Because of their expertise and specialized training, they may need little or no specific direction in their daily activities. When examining the factor of control, it is necessary to focus on both the payer's control over the worker's daily activities and the payer's influence over the worker.

    Payer's right to exercise control

    It is the right of the payer to exercise control that is relevant, not whether the payer actually exercises this right.

    It is the control of a payer over a worker that is relevant and not the control of a payer over the end result of a product or service purchased.

    Examples of questions the CRA may ask you to determine the status

    Duties

    • Did the worker have defined duties?
    • Could the payer assign or change the worker's duties at their discretion including nature and volume of workload?
    • Could the worker refuse work from the payer without ramifications?

    Training

    • Did the payer provide the worker with training? If yes, what is the nature and the duration of the training?
    • Was the worker required to pay for the training?
    • Where was the training held - on site or off site? If off site, did the payer provide the worker with transportation, accommodation and or meal allowances?
    • Was the worker paid for the time they spent in training?

    Work schedule

    • Does the worker have set days and hours of work?
    • If the worker's days and hours of work are flexible, are they subject to the approval of the payer?
    • Is the worker required to record they days and hours of work, for example: by email, timesheets, timecards?
    • What is the reason for keeping track of the worker's days and hours of work?
    • Can the worker leave at anytime during the day without the approval of the payer?
    • If the worker is unable to work due to illness or family emergencies? Is the worker required to notify the payer?
    • If the worker wishes to take time off, is the worker required to ask for the payer's approval?

    Location of services

    • Is the worker required to work at the payer's office? If no, could the worker perform their service at another location of their choosing without the payer's approval?

    Manner in which work was to be done

    • Does the payer have first call/priority of the worker's time?
    • Who set the worker's deadlines and priorities?
    • Does the worker have the option to choose whatever method they feel appropriate to finish the task? If yes, is it subject to the payer's approval?

    Degree of autonomy

    • Did the worker work under the supervision of the payer or the staff of the payer?
    • How often did the worker report to the supervisor - daily, weekly, etc.? Is it via telephone, text, email or in person?
    • Is the worker's work subject to review by the payer/supervisor? If yes, how often?
    • Does the payer have the right to direct the worker to redo the work? If yes, would the worker be compensated for the time to redo the work?
    • Is the worker required to attend meetings with the payer? If yes, how often are these meetings held?
    • What is the purpose of these meetings? Other than the worker and the payer, who else are present during these meetings?

    Remuneration

    • Who determined the worker's rate of pay? Is it negotiated between the worker and the payer?
    • Is the income guaranteed regardless of volume of work performed?
    • Who determined the frequency and method of the worker's remuneration?
    • How often is the worker paid?

    Exclusivity

    • Can the worker accept work from other competitors of the payer? If yes, is it subject to the payer's approval?

    Right to exercise punitive action and or terminate the relationship

    • Can the payer exercise disciplinary actions against the worker?
    • Did the payer have a right to dismiss the worker without cause and notice?
    • What kind of circumstances can cause the relationship between the payer and the worker be terminated?

    • Indicators that the worker is an employee

      • The relationship is one of subordination. The payer will often direct, scrutinize, and effectively control many elements of how and when the work is carried out
      • The payer controls the worker with respect to both the results of the work and the method used to do the work
      • The payer chooses and controls the method and amount of pay. Salary negotiations may still take place in an employer-employee relationship
      • The payer decides what jobs the worker will do
      • The payer chooses to listen to the worker's suggestions but has the final word
      • The worker requires permission to work for other payers while working for this payer
      • Where the schedule is irregular, priority on the worker's time is an indication of control over the worker
      • The worker receives training or direction from the payer on how to do the work. The overall work environment between the worker and the payer is one of subordination

    • Indicators that the worker is self-employed

      • A self-employed individual usually works independently
      • The worker does not have anyone overseeing their activities
      • The worker is usually free to work when and for whom they choose and may provide their services to different payers at the same time
      • The worker can accept or refuse work from the payer
      • The working relationship between the payer and the worker does not present a degree of continuity, loyalty, security, subordination, or integration, all of which are generally associated with an employer-employee relationship

  4. Ownership of the tools and equipment

    Consider if the worker owns and provides tools and equipment to accomplish the work. Contractual control of, and responsibility for, an asset in a rental or lease situation is also considered under this factor.

    What is considered a significant investment

    What is relevant is the significant investment in the tools and equipment along with the cost of replacement, repair, and insurance. A worker who has made a significant investment is likely to retain a right over the use of these assets, diminishing the payer's control over how the work is carried out. In addition, such a significant investment may place the worker at a risk of a financial loss.

    Tools and equipment can vary widely in terms of value and can include everything from wrenches and hammers, to specialized clothing, appliances, stethoscopes, musical instruments, computers, and vehicles such as trucks and tractors.

    Self-employed individuals often supply the tools and equipment required for a contract. As a result, the ownership of tools and equipment by a worker is more commonly associated with a business relationship.

    However, employees sometimes also have to provide their own tools. The courts have acknowledged that because a worker is required to provide tools of the trade, this does not in itself mean that the worker is a self-employed individual. For example, many skilled tradespeople such as auto mechanics have to supply their own tools, even if they are full-time employees.

    Examples of questions the CRA may ask you to determine the status
    • What kind of tools and equipment are required for the worker to perform their service effectively?
    • Is it common in the industry for workers of either status to provide tools and equipment?
    • Did the payer provide any tools and equipment for the worker to use? If yes, did the payer charge the worker for the use of the tools and equipment provided?
    • What kind of tools and or equipment did the payer provide?
    • Did the worker provide any tools and equipment to perform their service? If yes, what kind of tools and equipment did the worker provide?
    • Is it a requirement or a preference of the worker to provide these tools and equipment?
    • What is the value of tools and equipment provided?
    • Who is responsible for the repair, replacement, maintenance and insurance of the tools and equipment?

    • Indicators that the worker is an employee

      • The payer supplies most of the tools and equipment the worker needs. In addition, the payer is responsible for repair, maintenance, and insurance costs
      • The payer retains the right of use over the tools and equipment provided to the worker
      • The worker supplies the tools and equipment and the payer reimburses the worker for their use

    • Indicators that the worker is self-employed

      • The worker provides the tools and equipment needed for the work. In addition, the worker is responsible for the costs of repairs, insurance, and maintenance to the tools and equipment
      • The worker has made a significant investment in the tools and equipment and the worker retains the right over the use of these assets
      • The worker supplies their own workspace, is responsible for the costs to maintain it, and does substantial work from that site

  5. Subcontracting work or hiring assistants

    Consider if the worker can subcontract work or hire assistants. This factor can help decide a worker's business presence because subcontracting work or hiring assistants can affect their chance of profit and risk of loss.

    Examples of questions the CRA may ask you to determine the status
    • Is the worker hired for their expertise or specific skills? If yes, what is the worker's area of expertise?
    • Can the worker subcontract the work without the approval of the payer? If yes, who would be responsible to pay the subcontractor?
    • Can the worker hire someone to assist them to do and or finish the work without the approval of the payer? If yes, who would be responsible to pay the assistant the worker hired? Who would determine the rate of pay of the assistant hired?

    • Indicators that the worker is an employee

      • The worker cannot hire helpers or assistants
      • The worker does not have the ability to hire and send replacements, the worker has to do the work personally

    • Indicators that the worker is self-employed

      • The worker does not have to carry out the services personally. They can hire another party to either do the work or help do the work, and pay the costs for doing so
      • The payer has no say in whom the worker hires

  6. Financial risk

    Consider the degree of financial risk taken by the worker. Consider if there are any fixed ongoing costs incurred by the worker or any expenses that are not reimbursed.

    Employees will usually not have financial risks

    Usually, employees will not have any financial risk as any expenses will be reimbursed, and they will not have fixed ongoing costs.

    Self-employed individuals, on the other hand, can have financial risk and incur losses because they usually pay fixed monthly costs even if work is not currently being done.

    Employees and self-employed individuals may be reimbursed for business or travel expenses. Therefore, focus on the expenses that are not reimbursed by the payer.

    Examples of questions the CRA may ask you to determine the status
    • Does the payer reimburse the worker for any expenses? Are these expenses reimbursed as part of negotiated benefits?
    • Does the worker have copies of any financial statements showing expenses related to their work?
    • Who covers the cost for travel expenses, benefits, vacation pay, and certain leaves?
    • Does the worker have any expenses that could be so substantial as to incur a business loss?
    • Does the worker have any expenses pertaining to the running of a business, for example: rent, advertising, business telephone, overhead?
    • How is the remuneration calculated? What does it include? Is the remuneration based on seniority? Is it based by the hour or by a lump sum?
    • Is the worker liable for any expenses if they cannot perform the work?
    • Can the worker look for work with other payers?
    • Can the worker hire any helpers or assistants to do some portions of their work? Who pays the helpers? If the worker hires the helpers, is the payer's approval necessary?
    • Can the worker be liable for negligence or for inappropriate actions in the course of their work?
    • Can the payer discharge the worker at any time without incurring a liability?
    • Can the worker terminate the services at any time without incurring a liability? Is the worker entitled to terminate the services at any time? Can they be liable for damages?

    • Indicators that the worker is an employee

      • The worker is not usually responsible for any operating expenses
      • Generally, the working relationship between the worker and the payer is continuous
      • The worker is not financially liable if they do not fulfil the obligations of the contract
      • The payer chooses and controls the method and amount of pay, salary negotiations may still take place in an employer-employee relationship

    • Indicators that the worker is self-employed

      • The worker hires helpers to assist in the work. The worker pays the hired helpers
      • The worker does a substantial amount of work from their own workspace and incurs expenses relating to the operation of that workspace
      • The worker is hired for a specific job rather than an ongoing relationship
      • The worker is financially liable if they do not fulfil the obligations of the contract
      • The worker does not receive any protection or benefits from the payer
      • The worker advertises and actively markets their services

  7. Responsibility for investment and management

    Consider the degree of responsibility for investment and management held by the worker. A significant investment is evidence that a business relationship may exist. You should also consider if the worker is free to make business decisions that affect their profit or loss.

    Examples of questions the CRA may ask you to determine the status
    • Does the worker have an operating name? If yes, is it incorporated? If yes, you may need to provide a copy of the certificate of incorporation.
    • Does the worker maintain a business office either at their home or a commercial location?
    • Is there a separate phone line, fax line, modem line dedicated to the worker's services/business?
    • Does the worker have a business bank account?
    • Does the worker have pre-printed invoices, letterheads or business cards?
    • Does the worker advertise their services for hire, for example: in periodicals, websites, magazines, newspapers?
    • Does the worker carry their own liability insurance for errors and omissions?
    • Does the worker maintain books and records for the services provided? This refers to keeping a record of revenues generated and expenses paid for the services performed, for example:invoices, purchase receipts, business bank statements
    • Does the worker manage their own staff?
    • Does the worker hire individuals to help them to perform the work and the worker pays these individuals?
    • Is the worker registered with CRA as a business, that is, was the worker assigned a business number?
    • Does the worker issue invoices to the payer for payments? If yes, does the worker charge the payer GST/HST for the services rendered?
    • How did the worker report the income earned on their T1 personal income tax return - business or employment income? If the worker reports income earned as business income, did they claim any business expenses?

    • Indicators that the worker is an employee

      • The worker has no capital investment in the payer's business
      • The worker does not have a business presence

    • Indicators that the worker is self-employed

      • The worker has capital investment
      • The worker manages their staff
      • The worker hires and pays individuals to help do the work
      • The worker has established a business presence
      • The worker has significant investment, consider if the worker is free to make business decisions that affect their profit or loss

  8. Opportunity for profit

    Consider whether the worker can realize a profit or incur a loss, as this indicates that a worker controls the business aspects of services rendered and that a business relationship likely exists. To have a chance of a profit and a risk of a loss, a worker has to have potential proceeds and expenses, and one could exceed the other.

    Consider this factor from the worker's perspective

    This factor has to be considered from the worker's perspective, not the payer's. It is for the most part an assessment of the degree to which the worker can control their proceeds and expenses.

    Employees normally do not have the chance of a profit and risk of a loss even though their remuneration can vary depending on the terms of their employment contracts. For example, employees working on a commission or piece-rate basis, or employees with a productivity bonus clause in their contract can increase their earnings based on their productivity. This increase in income is not normally viewed as a profit, as it is not the excess of proceeds over expenses.

    Employees may have expenses directly related to their employment, such as automobile expenses, and board and lodging costs. Normally, expenses would not place employees at risk of incurring a loss because it is unlikely that the expenses would be greater than their remuneration.

    Self-employed individuals normally have the chance of profit or risk of loss, because they have the ability to pursue and accept contracts as they see fit. They can negotiate the price (or unilaterally set their prices) for their services and have the right to offer those services to more than one payer. Self-employed individuals will normally incur expenses to carry out the terms and conditions of their contracts, and to manage those expenses to maximize net earnings. Self-employed individuals can increase their proceeds and/or decrease their expenses in an effort to increase profit.

    Employees generally do not share in profits or suffer losses incurred by the payer's business.

    The method of payment may help to decide if the worker has the opportunity to make a profit or incur a loss. In an employer-employee relationship, the worker is normally guaranteed a return for the work done and is usually paid on an hourly, daily, weekly, or similar basis.

    Similarly, some self-employed individuals are paid on an hourly basis. However, when a worker is paid a flat rate for the work done, it generally indicates a business relationship, especially if the worker incurs expenses in doing the work.

    Examples of questions the CRA may ask you to determine the status
    • Is the work relationship continuous in nature, which is, is the work guaranteed?
    • Is the worker's compensation guaranteed as long as he or she works?
    • Is the worker paid a fixed amount for a specific end result with a deadline? What is the worker's rate of pay - hourly/weekly/piece work?
    • Is the worker financially liable if they do not fulfill the obligations of the contract, for example: the worker does not meet the deadline?
    • If the work performed has be redone due to errors or omissions, will the worker be compensated to redo the work?
    • Is the worker responsible for all expenses incurred in the performance of the work?
    • What kind of expenses is the worker responsible for?
      • tools and equipment
      • home office such as utilities, phone, computer, printer, supplies
      • vehicle
      • insurance
      • accounting and advertising fees
      • travel
    • Are any of the expenses incurred reimbursed by the payer?
    • Who provides the guarantee to the clients for the work completed?
    • If the worker can hire assistant or subcontract the work, who determined the rate of pay of the assistant hired or the subcontractor?
    • Is the worker able to perform services for other clients while working for the payer?

    • Indicators that the worker is an employee

      • The worker is not normally in a position to realize a business profit or loss
      • The worker is entitled to benefit plans that are normally offered only to employees. These include registered pension plans, and group accident, health, and dental insurance plans

    • Indicators that the worker is self-employed

      • The worker can hire a substitute and the worker pays the substitute
      • The worker is compensated by a flat fee and incurs expenses in carrying out the services
