Gather the facts

Answer a few questions based on the following factors to help you decide the worker classification.

The results from each question need to be viewed as a whole in order to help you decide if an employee or a self-employed person.

Factors

  1. Contract arrangement

    The CRA asks the worker and the payer what their intent was when they entered into the working arrangement. Workers and payers can choose how they set up their affairs. However, the employment status they choose must reflect their working relationship.

    Factor 1 - Question and indicator

    At the time of contracting, how did the parties intend to characterize the relationship?

    • Employee-employer
    • Self-employed

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The two parties intend to enter into a contract of service (COS)

    If the contract of service is in writing, it normally contains information such as:

    • Scale or rate of pay
    • Hours of work
    • Location of work
    • Period of employment
    Example

    Heather is starting work for Robert, who owns the corner store in her neighborhood. They both agreed on the telephone that she would work from 7:30AM to 4:30PM, Monday to Friday, earning $10 an hour.

    Robert and Heather have entered into a valid verbal contract of service and, as a result, they have an employer-employee relationship.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The two parties intend to enter into a contract for services (CFS).

    Example

    Paul operates a business under the name of Paul’s Beautiful Gardens. He advertises his services in the spring for work during the summer months.

    IT Computers Ltd. hires Paul’s services under a written agreement for a period of 3 months. Paul’s Beautiful Gardens will maintain the lawns and shrubs for a monthly fee of $2,000.

    In this case, Paul has an agreement to perform specific work for IT Computers Ltd. in return for payment. Paul has entered into a valid contract for services with IT Computers Ltd. and is considered a self-employed individual.

  5. Responsibility for investment and management

    Consider the degree of responsibility for investment and management held by the worker. A significant investment is evidence that a business relationship may exist. You should also consider if the worker is free to make business decisions that affect their profit or loss.

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker performs their services independently, for their own business.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker provides their services under the name of the payer.

    It is also possible the person is operating as an incorporated employee. Learn more: Personal services business.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker has a business presence that is separate from the payer’s business.

    • No

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

  6. Opportunity for profit (incorporated employee (such as PSBs) or a self-employed through their own corp)

    Consider whether the worker can realize a profit or incur a loss, as this indicates that a worker controls the business aspects of services rendered and that a business relationship likely exists. To have a chance of a profit and a risk of a loss, a worker has to have potential proceeds and expenses, and one could exceed the other.

    Consider this factor from the worker's perspective

    This factor has to be considered from the worker's perspective, not the payer's. It is for the most part an assessment of the degree to which the worker can control their proceeds and expenses.

    Employees normally do not have the chance of a profit and risk of a loss even though their remuneration can vary depending on the terms of their employment contracts. For example, employees working on a commission or piece-rate basis, or employees with a productivity bonus clause in their contract can increase their earnings based on their productivity. This increase in income is not normally viewed as a profit, as it is not the excess of proceeds over expenses.

    Employees may have expenses directly related to their employment, such as automobile expenses, and board and lodging costs. Normally, expenses would not place employees at risk of incurring a loss because it is unlikely that the expenses would be greater than their remuneration.

    Self-employed individuals normally have the chance of profit or risk of loss, because they have the ability to pursue and accept contracts as they see fit. They can negotiate the price (or unilaterally set their prices) for their services and have the right to offer those services to more than one payer. Self-employed individuals will normally incur expenses to carry out the terms and conditions of their contracts, and to manage those expenses to maximize net earnings. Self-employed individuals can increase their proceeds and/or decrease their expenses in an effort to increase profit.

    Employees generally do not share in profits or suffer losses incurred by the payer's business.

    The method of payment may help to decide if the worker has the opportunity to make a profit or incur a loss. In an employer-employee relationship, the worker is normally guaranteed a return for the work done and is usually paid on an hourly, daily, weekly, or similar basis.

    Similarly, some self-employed individuals are paid on an hourly basis. However, when a worker is paid a flat rate for the work done, it generally indicates a business relationship, especially if the worker incurs expenses in doing the work.

    Factor 7 - Questions and indicators

    Is the work relationship continuous in nature, which is, is the work guaranteed?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    An employee is generally hired on a continuous basis.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker must find contracts by themselves and the payer is not responsible if there is no work available.

    Is the worker's compensation guaranteed as long as they work?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker’s income is guaranteed. It is not based on the fulfilment of the contract agreed between the parties.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker’s income is not guaranteed. It is based on the fulfilment of the contract agreed between the parties.

    Is the worker paid a fixed amount for a specific end result with a deadline?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker is guaranteed a fixed remuneration regardless of the volume of work performed.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    A self-employed person is generally hired to work on a defined task. The only commitment from the worker is on the end result of the agreed task.

    Is the worker financially liable if they do not fulfill the obligations of the contract (for example: the worker does not meet the deadline)?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer is financially responsible for the costs related to completion of the contract.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker is financially responsible for the costs related to completion of the contract.

    If the work performed has be redone due to errors or omissions, will the worker be compensated to redo the work?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker is guaranteed remuneration, generally based on time, even if the payer requires them to redo a task due to errors or omissions.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker is generally paid based on task completion, there would be no additional compensation to correct errors or omissions, unless negotiated within the contract.

    Is the worker responsible for all expenses incurred in the performance of the work (for example: tools and equipment, home office, vehicle, insurance)?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer is responsible to pay expenses incurred in the performance of the work.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker is responsible to pay expenses incurred in the performance of the work.

    Does the worker provide the guarantee to the clients for the work completed?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer provides guarantees to the client for the quality and that the work is completed.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker provides guarantees to the client for the quality and that the work is completed.

    If the worker can hire assistant or subcontract the work, can the worker determine the rate of pay of the assistant hired or the subcontractor?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The worker would not be able to hire assistants or helpers, the payer would assign additional resources if needed and the payer would also determine the helpers rates of pay.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker sets the rates for any assistants or subcontractors they hire, and is responsible for paying them.

    Is the worker able to perform services for other clients while working for the payer?

    • Yes
    • No

    Indicator: Worker is an employee

    The payer would have first call on the worker’s time.

    Indicator: Worker is self-employed

    The worker would set their own schedule and work priorities as they see fit.

Analyze the facts

You need to analyze the facts by organizing and assessing the information you gathered. This process will move the worker into one side of the continuum, and confirm if there is an employment relationship, or if the worker is in business on his own account.

You must analyze the answer to each question from both the payer and the worker to help you decide if the evidence moves the worker to the employee or self-employed side of the continuum.

  • Worker is an employee
  • Worker is self-employed

Weighing the facts

This is the most important part of analyzing the information. When reviewing the factors, there may be some facts that support an employee-employer relationship and other facts indicate self-employement.

To help you resolve this issue, you must determine the true essence of the working relationship of the parties. You must review the indicators in the factors of control, ownership of tools and equipment, opportunity for profit and financial risk, and responsibility for investment and management. As you respond to each question you should be able to decide what weight to assign each category based on a thorough review of all the responses. For example, control may favour an employee relationship, but ownership of tools may be assigned more weight due to how the total relationship is structured. This may be an indicator because of the increased weight given to this factor, the worker is moved into the independent contractor side of the continuum.

Case-by-case basis

Whether a worker is an employee or self-employed needs to be analyzed on a case-by-case basis. Each case is unique and requires a thorough review of the facts. It is also important to remember that when attempting to move a worker to one side of the continuum or the other, you must examine the total working relationship. There is no one factor that is determinative, and there is a non-exhaustive list of factors to consider.

Consider special situations

A deemed employer is one of the following:

  • a third party that generally controls, directly supervises, or pays the remuneration to an insured person in its own name and not on behalf of the actual employer.
  • a person who has rights or privileges on premises or property under a licence, permit, or agreement and who agrees to allow another employer's workers to work on these premises or property.

Analyzing the relationship:

  • who supervises the worker?
  • where do the workers perform their tasks?
  • does the party paying the salary act in its own name?
  • what is the procedure for issuing pay?
  • where do the funds necessary for the payment of the salaries come from?
